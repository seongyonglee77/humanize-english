## Positive Style Patterns from Published Papers

This note identifies what the author **does naturally** in three published papers:

- `assets/sample1.md` - *Computers & Education*
- `assets/sample2.md` - *Lingua*
- `assets/sample3.md` - *Applied Linguistics Review*

All observations below are based on those samples only.

---

## 1. Sentence Length Profile

- **Estimated average sentence length:** about **28-32 words**.
- **Typical range:** about **12-45 words**.
- **Pattern:** a **mix of medium and long sentences**, with shorter sentences used for pivots, claims, or section-level emphasis.

### Evidence

- **Shorter framing sentence:** “**Therefore, training EFL teachers to notice and utilize ICT affordances emerging from language teaching contexts has been addressed as an important issue in CALL-based teacher education**” (sample1).
- **Medium sentence:** “**Therefore, all administrative positions were filled with bilingual speakers of the two languages**” (sample2).
- **Long sentence:** “**To fill this gap in the literature, drawing on affordance theory ... the present study is an investigation of the extent to which the ELF-related social environment ... may support Chinese students' use of ELF for intercultural communications**” (sample2).

Overall, the author **often** builds paragraphs with one or two long analytical sentences supported by shorter interpretive or transition sentences.

## 2. Dominant Sentence Architectures

### Pattern A. Concessive lead + main claim

The author **often uses** concession before the main claim.

- “**Although numerous studies have reported educational benefits of virtual environment (VE), little is known about whether ...**” (sample1)
- “**Although the students were exposed to different varieties of English ..., many of them still maintained native-speaker-centric views ...**” (sample2)
- “**However, previous research does not extensively investigate ... This study therefore examined ...**” (sample3)

This pattern appears with **Although**, **However**, and sometimes **While**.

### Pattern B. Participial/opening prepositional frame

The author **often uses** fronted openings to set theoretical or procedural context.

- “**Drawing on affordance theory, this study is an exploration of how socioenvironmental factors ...**” (sample2)
- “**Drawing on previous research on the ecological association of agency and ICT affordances ..., the study adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research approach ...**” (sample1)
- “**Given that diverse factors have been found to influence perceptions of ELF, a paucity of research has been conducted ...**” (sample3)

### Pattern C. Gap-then-contribution move

This is one of the author's **most consistent** research-writing moves.

- “**However, previous studies on EFL teacher awareness of technology affordances focus on only a few constructs of agency ... Thus, this study suggests the synthesis of qualitative evidence (SQD) strategies ...**” (sample1)
- “**However, to date, there has been little discussion about ... To fill this gap in the literature ... the present study is an investigation ...**” (sample2)
- “**To date, however, two important gaps have remained unfilled ... In response to the gaps, the study is to provide the integrative model ...**” (sample3)

### Pattern D. Colon elaboration

The author **often uses** a colon to move from a general claim to a list, category, or reformulation.

- “**The original model suggested seven key strategies: (1) alignment of theory and practice ...**” (sample1)
- “**interview data to illustrate emerging themes in four domains of affordances: 1) existing affordances, 2) perceived affordances ...**” (sample2)
- “**Findings from those studies provide an important basis for current issues in HE: (1) pedagogy ... (2) HE language policy ... (3) HE management ...**” (sample3)

### Pattern E. Former/latter comparison

The author **occasionally uses** explicit former/latter contrast rather than constant synonym variation.

- “**while the former was perceived as an instant mode of resources delivery, the latter was carried out by the permanent embedding of learning materials into virtual objects**” (sample1)
- “**whereas the former ' adheres to strict native norms,' whereas the latter ' offers an opportunity for learner agency ...'**” (sample2)
- “**the former was slightly more influential to perceived behavioural control than the latter**” (sample3)

### Pattern F. Demonstrative follow-up sentence

The author **often uses** a finding sentence followed by a demonstrative interpretation.

- “**The findings indicate that ...**” (sample1)
- “**This study confirms the importance of international faculty and students ...**” (sample2)
- “**This result indicates that subjective norm was the most significant predictor ...**” (sample3)

### Pattern G. Purpose statement before method/action

The author **often uses** purpose frames before describing method or design.

- “**For this purpose, we adopted a qualitative-dominant research approach ...**” (sample1)
- “**By doing so, the authors aim to observe how VE affordances emerge ...**” (sample1)
- “**To fill this gap in the literature ... the present study is an investigation ...**” (sample2)

### For describing participant actions

The author **often uses** action verbs tied to agency.

### For describing relationships

## 4. Preferred Transition Phrases

The author prefers a small, reusable transition set rather than constant variation.

### Adding

- **often:** “**Also,**” - “**Also, using 'portal' or 'invisible mode', I can quickly move ...**” (sample1)
- **often:** “**In addition,**” - “**In addition, EMI affordances were institutionally provided**” (sample2)
- **occasionally:** “**It further ...**” - “**It further investigated how the participants gained benefits ...**” (sample1)
- **rarely:** “**Additionally**” appears once in sample2, but the papers more often use **Also** or **In addition**.

### Contrasting

- **very often:** “**However,**” - abundant across all three samples.
- **often:** “**Unlike ...**” - “**Unlike Zoom, the VE platform shows the movement of a teacher and students ...**” (sample1)
- **often:** “**Despite ...**” - “**Despite the reported importance of VE design ... there is still no empirical research ...**” (sample1)
- **occasionally-to-often:** “**While ...**” - “**While affordances can come from social ... as well as physical ...**” (sample2)

### Cause / result

- **very often:** “**Thus,**” - common across all samples.
- **very often:** “**Therefore,**” - common across all samples.
- **often:** “**In this regard,**” - “**In this regard, teacher agency is manifested in such relational contexts ...**” (sample1)
- **often:** “**Given that ...**” - “**Given that diverse factors have been found to influence perceptions of ELF ...**” (sample3)

### Reformulation

- **often:** “**In other words,**” - sample1, sample3
- **occasionally:** “**Put it another way,**” - sample1
- **occasionally:** “**that is to say,**” - “**that is to say, satisfying the validation ... through 'data convergence'**” (sample1)

### Specification

- **often:** “**In particular,**” - common in all three samples
- **often:** “**Specifically,**” - sample1
- **occasionally:** “**In this vein,**” - “**In this vein, many participants (n = 49) identified ...**” (sample1)

### Purpose

- **often:** “**For this purpose,**” - sample1
- **often:** “**By doing so,**” - sample1
- **not found in these three samples:** “**To this end,**”

### Basis / framing

- **very often:** “**Drawing on ...**” - all three samples
- **occasionally:** “**Based on ...**” - “**Based on the findings ...**” (sample1)
- **often:** “**Guided by ...**” - “**Guided by previous research ... the authors noted three language-related affordances ...**” (sample2)

## 6. Absent or Rare Patterns

This section separates items that are **confirmed absent/rare** from items that are **not supported as absent** by the samples.

### Confirmed absent in the three samples

These forms were **not found** in the three sample papers:

- **delve**
- **leverage**
- **robust**
- **groundbreaking**
- **ever-evolving landscape**
- **In light of**
- **Together,** as a sentence transition

### Rare, not preferred defaults

These appear **once or only marginally**, so they do not look like default habits:

- **“It is important to note that”** - found in sample2 discussion once
- **“Additionally”** - appears in sample2, but much less often than **Also** or **In addition**

### Not absent enough to classify as non-style

These were **actually used often enough** in the samples that they should **not** be treated as absent style markers:

- **various**
- **significant**

So the validator should be careful: these two may be overused in some contexts, but they are **present in the author's published prose**.

## 7. Voice Distribution

### Describing previous studies: mostly active

The author **often uses active voice** when attributing prior work.

- “**Lan (2015) argued that ...**” (sample1)
- “**Canagarajah (2007) noted that ...**” (sample2)
- “**Jenkins (2014) argued for accommodating ...**” (sample3)

### Describing this study: active

The author **usually uses active voice** for study purpose, method, and claims.

- “**We adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research approach ...**” (sample1)
- “**the present study is an investigation of ...**” (sample2)
- “**the authors adopted the TPB as the basic theoretical framework ...**” (sample3)

### Describing general truths / theoretical claims: mixed, often impersonal or passive-like

The author **often shifts to impersonal academic voice**.

- “**only perceived affordances can be actualized ...**” (sample2)
- “**ELF communication is regarded as social practice ...**” (sample3)

### Describing procedures: often passive

Procedure writing is **often passive or semi-passive**.

- “**A purposive sampling method was used to recruit 78 EFL pre-service teachers ...**” (sample1)
- “**The recordings were then transcribed and translated into English ...**” (sample2)
- “**Structural equation modelling (SEM) was used ...**” (sample3)

### Describing data and findings: active/mixed

Data sentences are **mixed**, but active reporting verbs are common.

- “**The findings reported four pedagogical affordances ...**” (sample1)
- “**The interviews indicated that ...**” (sample2)
- “**The results indicate that ...**” (sample3)

## 8. Paragraph Structure

### 3-layer structure: topic sentence -> development -> closure

This pattern appears **often**.

Example from sample2 Section 5.4:

1. **Topic sentence:** “**Guided by affordance theory ... we identified participants' engagement and agency in ELF communication ...**”
2. **Development:** explanation plus extracts 11-16
3. **Closure:** “**Overall, the above analysis suggests that ...**”

Example from sample1 Section 4.1.2:

1. **Topic sentence:** “**Some participants reported they had experienced some technical limitations ...**”
2. **Development:** extracts 4-6 and interpretation
3. **Closure/forward move:** transition into the next affordance category

### Key terms are deliberately repeated

The author **often repeats core terms** instead of forcing synonym variation.

- sample1 repeatedly uses **VE affordances**, **agency**, **noticing**, **utilizing**
- sample2 repeatedly uses **affordances**, **ELF communication**, **multilingual environment**
- sample3 repeatedly uses **attitude**, **subjective norm**, **behavioural intention**, **ELF communication**

Example:

- “**VE affordances**” appears throughout sample1 rather than being constantly replaced.
- “**ELF communication**” is repeated across sample2 and sample3 as the stable organizing term.

### Demonstrative reference is common

The author **often uses** demonstratives to tie interpretation back to prior sentences.

- “**This finding corresponds to current research ...**” (sample1)
- “**This study confirms the importance ...**” (sample2)
- “**This result indicates that ...**” (sample3)
- “**In this regard, ...**” (sample1)

## Hedging Profile

The author's prose is analytical but not absolute. Hedging is **frequent**.

### Common hedges

- **may** - “**The theory of affordances may provide a theoretical framework ...**” (sample2)
- **can** - “**only perceived affordances can be actualized ...**” (sample2)
- **often** - “**L2 confidence in ELF communication has been often mentioned ...**” (sample3)
- **relatively** - “**participants from this university were relatively diverse ...**” (sample3)
- **potentially** - “**the SQD strategies as a unified pre-service teacher-training model that potentially enhances ...**” (sample1)

### Overall profile

- **Often uses:** may, can, often, relatively, potentially
- **Usually avoids:** absolute, unqualified certainty when making interpretations
- **Effect:** claims are usually framed as plausible, contextual, and evidence-bound rather than universal.

---

## Practical Takeaways for Option B

The validator/rewriter should preserve these high-probability style habits:

1. **Gap -> contribution** movement is central.
2. **Concessive openings** are normal in the author's published prose.
3. **Theory-framing openings** such as “Drawing on ...” and “Given that ...” are natural.
4. **Core terms are repeated intentionally**; do not over-synonymize.
5. **Agency / affordance / communication** verbs are strong style markers.
6. **Hedging is frequent and authentic**.
7. **The author prefers a controlled transition inventory**: However, Thus, Therefore, In this regard, In particular, Drawing on.
