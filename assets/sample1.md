## 1. Introduction

As computer-assisted language learning (CALL) has received increasing attention in English as a foreign language (EFL) teaching, it becomes more important than ever to prepare EFL pre-service teachers with new technology (Haines, 2015; Jeong, 2017). However, pre-service teachers, who do not have sufficient teaching opportunities, experience challenges in CALL-based teaching when CALL training courses are not properly designed (Al-Furaih, 2017; Nami, 2022). Thus, given the informed benefits of incorporating CALL into EFL classrooms, how to prepare EFL pre-service teachers with technological tools still remains significant for the success of EFL teacher training programs (Tondeur et al., 2020).

## A B S T R A C T

Although numerous studies have reported educational benefits of virtual environment (VE), little is known about whether the design-based learning project of developing VEs contributes to preservice  EFL  teachers ' noticing  and  utilizing  of  its  pedagogical  affordances.  Drawing  on  the affordance  theory  and  the  synthesis  of  qualitative  evidence  (SQD),  this  study  first  aimed  to explore the pedagogical affordances of VEs noticed by 78 Korean EFL pre-service teachers. It further investigated how the participants gained benefits in noticing and using these affordances from the SQD-based VE development project. We adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research approach with multiple data sources, such as interviews, group discussions, reflective journals, and  a  survey.  The  findings  reported  four  pedagogical  affordances,  including  communication, engaging  learning  environment,  constructability,  and  multimodal  sharing.  The  findings  also showed that the participants positively evaluated the SQD strategies for noticing and utilizing those affordances in their projects by activating their teacher agency. The study suggests the SQD strategies  for  the  interactive  learning  environment  in  which  EFL  pre-service  students  can  be trained to activate their agency in noticing and using ICT affordances. Academic and practical implications for pre-service teacher training and future research are suggested.

Although there exist many potential affordances of ICT tools, only certain affordances perceived by teachers can be realized into language teaching (Stoffregen, 2003; van Lier, 2004). In this regard, ' affordances are discovered through perceptual learning, and the effective use of affordances must also be learned ' in the ecology of teaching and learning (van Lier, 2008, p. 597). Therefore, training EFL teachers to notice and utilize ICT affordances emerging from language teaching contexts has been addressed as an important issue in CALL-based teacher education (Haines, 2015; Nami, 2022; White et al., 2021). Key to affordance perception is agency enactment in noticing and utilizing ICT affordances (Chen, 2022; Jeon et al., 2022; Kitade, 2015; White, 2018). The ecological view underscores the epistemological role of agency in affordance noticing, eschewing an ontological stance on affordance as being 'fi xed, predetermined attributes that will be automatically recognized by individuals ' (White et al., 2021, p. 93). Thus, CALL teacher-training courses need to provide pre-service teacher trainees with specific learning projects, and activities that can engage them in noticing potential affordances of ICT tools for language teaching (Liu &amp; Chao, 2018).

Foregoing studies on CALL have addressed how EFL teachers ' knowledge of ICT affordances develops as they exercise their teacher agency (e.g., Chen, 2022; Kitade, 2015; White et al., 2021). These studies offer diverse mediating factors that influence agency exertion, including collaborative work (Haines, 2015; White et al., 2021), reflective teaching (Chen, 2022; Kitade; 2015), pedagogical beliefs  (Chen,  2022;  Kitade,  2015),  and  social  situations  (Chen,  2022).  However,  previous  studies  on  EFL  teacher  awareness  of technology affordances focus on only a few constructs of agency respectively, not providing a unified model that addresses those factors in a single context. Also, these studies have paid attention only to in-service teachers ' experiences of using technology in their classrooms. Thus, this study suggests the synthesis of qualitative evidence (SQD) strategies as a unified pre-service teacher-training model that potentially enhances their experiences of technology integration (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016).

The SQD model has integrated seven qualitative methods that have been proven to be effective in training pre-service teachers with technology  in  their  teaching  practices  (Tondeur  et  al.,  2012,  2016).  Research  notes  that  the  SQD  model  that  is  guided  by  the constructivist approach, such as active learning (Al-Furaih, 2017; Campbell et al., 2020) and design-based learning (Jeong, 2017; Lan, 2015), retains potential benefits in enhancing their content and technological awareness (As ¸ık et al., 2020; Tondeur et al., 2016, 2020), as this approach facilitates student teachers ' autonomy, engagement, and self-efficacy to become active learners rather than passive recipients of fixed knowledge in teacher training (Ho et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2010). Based on this potential, this study explores how the SQD-strategies provide EFL student teachers with active and learner-directed environments that may invite them to activate their agency in noticing ICT affordances (van Lier, 2008).

A customizable 2D virtual environment (VE) platform was selected as a technological tool in this study for the following three reasons. First, despite the numerous benefits of VE for language learning reported in the literature (Bahari, 2022; Ciekanski et al., 2021; Nocchi, 2018), how to train EFL pre-service teachers to notice and utilize its pedagogical affordances has been less explored. In fact, the trainability of VE has received renewed attention in education with the advent of AI-based virtual tools, such as virtual reality, augmented reality, and metaverse (Hwang &amp; Chien, 2022). Second, the VE platform has a high degree of designability: it allows pre-service teachers participants to build their own virtual classrooms for English teaching practices (Lan, 2015; Lan et al., 2018). In particular, this aspect is significant to this study because the technology design project reflects the constructivist perspective ingrained in the SQD model in ways to make pre-service teachers active learners (Campbell et al., 2020; Tondeur et al., 2012). Finally, the VE platform can be used as an appropriate technology to train pre-service teachers to exert their agency in online teaching (Huang et al., 2010; Levak &amp; Son, 2017). Given that a sudden shift to emergency online teaching during the COVID-19 pandemic has urged in-service teachers to enact their agency in response to educational challenges and pedagogical opportunities from ICT use, recent studies call for training language teachers with online technologies even in the post-pandemic classroom (Chen, 2022; Jeon et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2021).

Therefore, this study first aims to explore pedagogical affordances of the VE platform for English teaching as perceived by EFL preservice teachers when they develop their VEs guided by seven SQD strategies in a 16-week course. It further explores how these SQD strategies help them notice and exploit VE affordances for teaching practices by activating their agency. For this purpose, we adopted a qualitative-dominant research approach with multiple data sources to explore the participants ' voices from different angles (D ¨ ornyei, 2007), referring to research on ICT affordance, active learning, and SQD-based teacher-training (e.g., Campbell et al., 2020; Jeon et al., 2022; Nami, 2022). The study conceptualizes EFL pre-service teachers as both learners and teachers as they learn language teaching methods in a teacher training program (Jeong, 2017; Kuure et al., 2016). Thus, the authors observe the processes by which the participants learned noticing, choosing, and exploiting VE affordances with English teaching goals in mind (Liu &amp; Chao, 2018).

## 2. Literature review

## 2.1. The affordance perspective on teacher training: the potential of the SQD model

The affordance theory provides an authentic explanation of how language teachers and learners perceive and act upon educational opportunities of technology in learning environments (Chen, 2022; Jeon, 2022). Drawing on Gibson ' s (1986) definition of affordance as possibilities for action, an epistemological view on affordance underscores an individual ' s agentic perception as a central psychological device for emerging affordances from the environment (Kitade, 2015; van Lier, 2008). In other words, although an environment invites an agent to its unlimited existing affordances, those affordances can be perceived, chosen, and used for future actions as long as an individual exercises agency according to one ' s beliefs and goals (Stoffregen, 2003). In pedagogical contexts, affordance perception and exploitation should be learned by teachers because affordances ' may not automatically be recognized ' nor realized as teaching without teacher agency (White et al., 2021, p. 103). Therefore, CALL pre-service teacher-training should address how to J. Jeon et al.

encourage them to activate their agency in noticing specific ICT affordances according to their teaching goals and beliefs (Chen, 2022; Liu &amp; Chao, 2018).

The social cognitive framework views agency as the aggregate of diverse components, including intentionality, forethoughts with goal-setting, self-reflectiveness, and self-regulation through perceived self-efficacy (Bandura, 1997, 2001). An individual, who sets up goals with forethoughts and reflects on their capacity in response to critical situations, may make decisions for future actions on their self-efficacy (Bandura, 2001). However, an individual ' s action is also influenced by collective agency when such self-efficacy is not available at the individual level. This aspect of agency is supported by the ecological perspective on agency and affordance (Priestley et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2021): agency is mediated by ' social, interactional, cultural, institutional and other contextual factors ' (van Lier, 2008, p. 171). In this regard, teacher agency is manifested in such relational contexts, the constructs of which include interactions between a teacher and students as well as collaborative work among students (White, 2018). Thus, by enacting agency based on one ' s pedagogical beliefs, a teacher evaluates and selects ICT affordances to make an informed decision for future teaching (Ashton, 2022; Jeon et al., 2022).

Given that the effective perception and utilization of affordances need to be learned (Liu &amp; Chao, 2018), training teachers to exercise their agency is one of the keys to enhancing their technological knowledge (Haines, 2015; Nami, 2022). In this regard, teacher educators should provide an active learning environment because ' learning requires active, perceiving agents engaging in activities, tasks,  and  projects  rather  than  passive  input  receivers  and  output  producers ' (Liu &amp; Chao,  2018,  p.  71).  For  this  purpose, teacher-training programs should support student teachers ' enhanced technological awareness by engaging them in these authentic activities  to  activate  their  agency  in  terms  of  its  aforementioned  constructs:  forethoughts  with  goal-setting,  self-regulation  and self-efficacy, collective agency, and self-reflectiveness (Jeong, 2017; Nami, 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). Although some aspects of agency have been tested by foregoing studies that explore the usefulness of diverse ICT-related tasks in a teacher education program from the constructivist perspective (e.g., Al-Furaih, 2017; Campbell et al., 2020; Nami, 2022), to date, no integrated model has been suggested to explore these agency constructs regarding EFL pre-service teacher training.

Observing the connection between these constructs of agency and the established qualitative methods to enhance technological knowledge, the authors suggest SQD as the integrated model to activate pre-service teachers ' agency to notice ICT affordances. Tondeur et al. (2012) put forward the SQD-strategies that synthesize diverse qualitative approaches to evaluate pre-service teacher education  programs  regarding  technology  integration.  The  micro-level  of  the  model,  namely  teacher  preparation,  is  frequently adopted  to  investigate  how  a  local  program  can  promote  self-efficacy  in  technological  skills,  engagement,  motivation,  and self-regulation (Tondeur et al., 2016). The original model suggested seven key strategies: (1) alignment of theory and practice in a training program (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016, 2020), (2) modeling technology use (As ¸ık et al., 2020), (3) reflection on the role of technology in education (Jeong, 2017; Ma et al., 2020), (4) design-based learning (Al-Furaih, 2017; Campbell et al., 2020), (5) collaborative work (Jeong, 2017; Ma et al., 2020), (6) authentic experiences in teaching (Cheong, 2010), and (7) continuous feedback (Al-Furaih, 2017; Campbell et al., 2020). These studies allude to the potential benefits of the SQD approach to develop pre-service teachers ' technological  awareness  by activating  their  agency  with  authentic,  active  learning  based  on  hands-on  practices  along with feedback from a teacher and peers (Young, 2004). Fig. 1 shows the exploratory conceptualization of the SQD-strategies, agency constructs, and affordance perception. However, despite such potential benefits of the SQD model to agency exertion as indicated in the literature, there is a dearth of empirical research that describes the dynamic interplay among the SQD-strategies, agency enactment, and affordance perception in pre-service teacher training.

In addition, most of the studies that adopted the SQD model primarily concentrated on the quantitative evaluation of teacher education programs, not describing the process by which the seven qualitative strategies in this model can contribute to enhancing EFL pre-service  teachers ' technological knowledge (e.g., As ¸ık  et  al.,  2020;  Howard  et  al.,  2021;  Tondeur  et  al.,  2016,  2020). ## 2.2. Affordances of VE for language learning

A virtual environment (VE) has been studied for the past two decades regarding its potential educational benefits in online teaching contexts (Cheong, 2010; Reiso ˘ glu et al., 2017). Research on language learning in VEs has addressed diverse issues, including verbal interactions with avatars, the promotion of language skills, and L2 motivation and self-confidence (Bahari, 2022; Gamage et al., 2011; Lan, 2015). For example, Chen (2018), exploring adult students ' task-based EFL learning in a multi-user virtual environment (MUVE) found that MUVE served as an effective context in which to implement collaborative language tasks.

VE affordances for language learning have been widely investigated in the literature. In their scoping review, Mantziou et al. (2018) identified six pedagogical affordances of MUVE: (1) free navigation, (2) creation in virtual design, (3) modeling and simulation, (4) multichannel communication, (5) collaboration (e.g., social interaction such as meetings and role-play), and (6) content presentation with interactive tools. Other studies reported its benefits in promoting language skills in diverse contexts (e.g., Bahari, 2022; Jabbari &amp; Eslami, 2019; Lan et al., 2018; Nocchi, 2018; Xu et al., 2020). According to Jabbari and Eslami (2019), its design feature supports social and affective affordance by lowering L2 anxiety. Nocchi (2018) added that this culturally-enriched environment affords L2 learners ' intercultural communication with speakers from different L1 backgrounds. Wang et al. (2020) attributed such enhanced communication skills to interactions among L2 users in VE supporting collaborative language activities, enhanced self-efficacy and motivation, and authentic verbal communication using avatars. In their qualitative research on three-dimensional (3D) VE, Lan et al. (2018) found the immersive and authentic features of VE aided the young special students ' vocabulary learning by providing safe learning environments that are not always available in their real life. The findings of the studies indicate that VEs support language learning in online teaching when EFL teachers ' technology use in virtual classrooms has been increasingly important in recent years during the COVID-19 pandemic (Chen, 2022).

The literature emphasizes the careful design of VE to maximize its pedagogical affordances (Lan et al., 2018; Levak &amp; Son, 2017). To support L2 learning, research conceptualizes teachers as ICT designers who should consider the match between its environmental affordances and pedagogical benefits (Ciekanski et al., 2021; Kuure et al., 2016). Lan (2015) argued that the design of authentic VEs would  support ' self-directing  learning  to  complement  regular  EEL  classes ' (p.  26).  She  suggested  four  core  functions  for  VE design-ability: individual-resources provision, adaptability in satisfying diverse pedagogical needs, scaffolding in collaborative work, and appropriate feedback. Ciekanski et al. (2021) highlighted the usefulness of a scenario-based approach to VE-based language teaching in teacher training. These studies indicate that training future teachers for VE development is significant to promoting their technological, pedagogical knowledge particularly in online teaching situations (Kuure et al., 2016; Lan et al., 2018).

However, despite the reported importance of VE design to make the most of its affordances (Lan, 2015; Levak &amp; Son, 2017), there is still no empirical research on how to raise pre-service teachers ' attention to VE affordances from the design perspective. Thus, this study explores how the seven SQD-strategies facilitate their agency in noticing and utilizing VE affordances in VE development projects. By doing so, the authors aim to observe how VE affordances emerge from the participants ' perceptions when they design and develop their virtual classrooms. The following research questions (RQ) guide the study:

1.  What pedagogical affordances of VEs emerged from EFL pre-service teachers ' VE development project guided by seven SQD strategies?
2.  In what aspects did the participants perceive the benefits of seven SQD strategies to notice and use those affordances in the project?

## 3. Method

Drawing on previous research on the ecological association of agency and ICT affordances (Ashton, 2022; Chen, 2022; Jeon et al., 2022), the constructivist view on active learning (Campbell et al., 2020; Nami, 2022), and qualitative methods in the SQD-model (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016), the study adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research approach that takes a qualitative approach as the main method to explore the participants ' in-depth voices and uses descriptive survey data to complement the qualitative one (D ¨ ornyei, 2007; Ho et al., 2011). As previous studies on teacher agency and ICT affordance focused on the emerging nature of qualitative data, the study centered on the descriptive, exploratory aspect to add new insights to the body of literature rather than the inference-based report (Bazeley, 2013).

## 3.1. Participants and context

A purposive sampling method was used to recruit 78 EFL pre-service teachers (17 male, 61 female) from two classes of one course in an English teacher education program at a Korean university (age: mean = 21.24, SD: 0.82), as the program adopted CALL as an essential part of its curriculum (Galloway, 2005). The participants reported that none of them had previous experiences of using VE platforms for personal or teaching purposes.

The course, Material Development and English Teaching Method, was designed according to the communicative language teaching J. Jeon et al.

(CLT) perspective for which Brown and Lee (2015) was used as a main textbook. It focused on how to make a language classroom interactive by using tools (e.g., technology), methods (e.g., group activities), and tasks (e.g., task-based instruction). In addition to those lectures, the course included lesson planning and micro-teaching for teaching practices.

## 3.2. Research design: SQD-based VE development project

VE development projects lasted for 16 weeks in the fall 2021 semester. Unlike Cheong (2010) and Jeong et al. (2021) that used ready-made VEs for teaching practices, the participants built their VEs in groups for EFL teaching purposes by considering VE design and developing teaching materials (Jeong, 2017; Lan, 2015; Nami, 2022). The project included collaborative lesson-planning and micro-teaching of their peers.

As Fig. 2 shows, the project was carried out in the classroom in three phases: preparation (2 weeks), VE development (12 weeks), and micro-teaching (2 weeks). The participants were first prepared for VE development, including information-sharing about the VE platform, lesson planning, and CLT-based English teaching. They were assigned into 20 groups in two classes, each of which has three to four members in it. Then, the VE development was implemented in three sessions: (1) draft for basic floor plans, (2) modification for interactive VEs, and (3) final products. At the end of each session, each group conducted mini-presentations about their VEs and obtained feedback from other groups and the instructor. In the final phase, each group conducted collaborative micro-teaching because VEs were developed mainly for online teaching, not for classroom-based teaching as in previous studies on VE-based language teaching (e.g, Cheong, 2010; Jeong et al., 2021). Also, VE affordances can be maximized when student teachers as trainees consider its virtual spaces, where they use their avatars for interactions with other avatars (Bahari, 2022; Wang et al., 2020). Other groups participated in micro-teaching as EFL students and provided feedback afterwards.

The seven SQD-strategies guided the VE development project (Fig. 2). First, concerning SQD one, the participants were offered lectures about how to develop their virtual classrooms and lesson plans from the CLT perspective (Brown &amp; Lee, 2015; Jeong, 2017). Then, the instructor provided model VEs and diverse sources of how to build VEs. Regarding Strategy three, they reflected on their building work in accordance with their teaching goals throughout the project. For Strategy four and five, the project was guided by collaborative work and designed-based learning for active knowledge production (Campbell et al., 2020; Nami, 2022). For SQD six, they practiced micro-teaching in their own virtual classrooms as part of online teaching practices. Finally, regarding SQD seven, students received continuous feedback in all stages from their instructor and peers.

Most of the recent studies used 3D VE platforms to test the potential of VEs in benefiting language learning (e.g., Ciekanski et al., 2021; Jeong et al., 2021; Lan et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020). It is known that 3D VEs provide more authentic environments for learning and socio-physical presence than 2D VEs (Cho et al., 2015; Dalgarno &amp; Lee, 2010). The immersive nature of 3D avatars may mediate better L2 communicative skills in real-life contexts than 2D ones (Lan &amp; Liao, 2018). Despite these relatively superior aspects of 3D VEs to 2D ones, the authors selected Gather, 2D VE platform, as an innovative technological tool for the following reasons. First, unlike the traditional 2D VE platforms, Gather is an immersive VE platform with multimodal cutting-edge technologies, such as 2D avatars combined with instant messaging and a synchronous videoconferencing tool (webcam). In particular, the webcam technology facilitates social presence for communicative interactions in a synchronous setting, compensating for the lack of physical presence and immersion in 2D environments (Guichon &amp; Cohen, 2014; Jauregi et al., 2011). ## 3.3. Data collection

We adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research approach in which multiple data sources were used for the data triangulation; that is to say, satisfying the validation of the mixed research data through ' data convergence ' (D ¨ ornyei, 2007, p. 165). For RQ 1 that focuses on pedagogical affordances of VEs emerging in the project, we used group discussions, semi-structured focus-group interviews, and  reflections.  To  answer  RQ  2,  which  explored  the  participants ' perceptions  regarding  the  benefits  of  the  SQD-strategies  to affordance noticing, we used a survey, interviews and reflections. Fig. 4 shows the data collection methods and instruments.

Qualitative data were collected during the three phases of the VE development project (Fig. 2). All groups were asked to audiorecord their discussions in Korean (67.4 h), which were then transcribed verbatim and translated into English. One-shot interviews were administered to all participants via Zoom in Korean after micro-teaching (23 -37 min by each group). Interviews were videorecorded, transcribed, and translated into English. Finally, the participants reported reflections in Korean on VE affordances, VE use for teaching, and the benefits of the SQD to their affordance noticing and utilizing. Their reports were also translated into English. Descriptive survey data, collected at the end of the course, explored their perceptions of the potential benefits of SQD strategies to their noticing and using VE affordances. Seven statements in the questionnaire relate to the usefulness of seven SQD-strategies (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016) and one statement explores an overall assessment of the project (Appendix C). A seven-point Likert scale was used to measure these eight constructs.

## 3.4. Data analysis

Thematic analysis was used to identify emerging themes from the qualitative data (Bazeley, 2013; Braun &amp; Clarke, 2006). We conducted initial coding to obtain extracts relevant to two core topics: VE affordances (Bahari, 2022; Gamage et al., 2011; Warburton, 2009) and the benefits of SQD strategies to enact agency and notice affordances (Nami, 2022; Tondeur et al., 2012). The codes with common themes were colligated into a single group. Finally, we confirmed a good fit between the coded themes and the transcribed data. For the validation of the data analysis and interpretation, we provided the separate and total number of codes, using their frequency and relative percentage, as previous studies visualized their multiple data sources (e.g., Harmandao ˘ glu Baz et al., 2018; Nami, 2022). According to the guideline to prevent coder bias and increase reliability in coding (Connor &amp; Joffe, 2020), inter-rater reliability was calculated among the three researchers (Harmandao ˘ glu Baz et al., 2018). Cohen ' s kappa values were 0.78 for discussions, 0.73 for reflections, and 0.80 for interviews, indicating substantial inter-rater reliability.

To code interview data into the categories of VE affordances for educational purposes, we adopted an integrated view of educational affordances (Bower &amp; Sturman, 2015). Criticizing the ontological perspective by which affordances are simply perceived as diverse functions of ICT tools and environments, they argued that there should not be a clear line drawn among technological, social and pedagogical affordances. Instead, all affordances can be categorized as pedagogical affordances or simply affordances as long as educational agents perceive them as providing educational benefits. Meanwhile, regarding the unified way to code agency enactment, we attempted to identify critical situations in which the participants set up goals, lead their own learning, conduct collaborative work, and reflect on their VE design and use (Ashton, 2022; Chen, 2022; Kitade, 2015). In sum, interview analysis focused on the potential connection among SQD strategies, agency enactment, and affordance noticing (see Fig. 1).

For survey data, we followed Ho et al. ' s (2011) use of descriptive statistics: they explored the effect of the design-based virtual museum project on students ' collaborative learning skills, adopting a mixed research method: qualitative data analysis and descriptive statistics concerning the participants ' perceptions. We also conducted descriptive analysis of the survey data, using mean scores and standardized  deviation,  to  explore  the  participants ' perceptions  regarding  the  potential  benefits  of  the  SQD-strategies  to  their affordance noticing and utilizing (Appendix C).

## 4.1.1. Communication

When the participants were asked to design their virtual classrooms for teaching practices, they looked unskilled to use this new technology. To tackle this challenge, most of them first attempted to create VEs that were similar to a traditional classroom, reflecting on its basic functions to realize what they planned in a lesson plan. In this vein, many participants (n = 49) identified its interactive environmentsz in which they could use both an avatar and a webcam for both verbal and non-verbal communication. One participant noticed its communicative benefits during his collaborative work on the basic floor design. Specifically, he described how his avatar could move around virtual classrooms for a video chat, supporting social presence for authentic communication (Extract 1). This way of authentic communication has been reported by previous studies that suggested authenticity in communication as L2 affordance of VE platforms (Bahari, 2022; Jauregi et al., 2011; Lan, 2015).

## 4.2. RQ2: perceived benefits of the SQD strategies to affordance noticing and utilizing

To answer RQ2, which addressed the participants ' perceptions of how the SQD-strategies benefited VE affordances noticing and utilizing, this section presents the findings in two ways: a descriptive report of the survey result and a list of coded themes from the qualitative data.

First, the survey result provides the participants ' perceptions in a qualitative manner. The descriptive statistics show that the VE development project was beneficial to their noticing and utilizing VE affordances for English teaching (Mean = 6.17, SD: 0.95) (see number 8 in Appendix C). Among the seven SQD strategies, VE use for micro-teaching gained the highest score (Mean = 6.28, SD: 0.85), followed by reflection on VEs (Mean = 6.04, SD: 0.99) and collaborative work (Mean = 6.02, SD: 1.07). Alignment of theory and practice received the lowest score although the responses were slightly positive (Mean = 5.15, SD: 0.76). The results indicate that EFL pre-service teachers in Korea generally acknowledged the benefits of the SQD-based VE development project to noticing and using VE affordances for English teaching. The qualitative data below offer coding themes that emerged from the participants ' in-depth voices.

## 4.2.1. Alignment of theory and practice

Qualitative data from interviews and reflections revealed 14 themes (two themes for each strategy) regarding the potential benefits of SQD strategies to affordance noticing and utilizing (see Appendix D). Regarding alignment of theory and practice, two themes were identified: CLT perspective (n = 15) and abstract concepts (n = 12). The participants noted the usefulness of lectures on the current perspectives regarding language teaching, one of which was the CLT perspective (Extract 10). However, some of them reported negative attitudes toward some abstract concepts (Extract 11), as it was perceived as challenging to actualize those concepts in VEs.

Model VEs offered by the instructor were perceived as beneficial for pre-service teachers, who had not had previous experiences of using the tool (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016). ## 4.2.7. Continuous feedback

Continuous feedback from an instructor and peers gained the most comments from the respondents (n = 66) as an effective strategy to enhance their noticing and using VE affordances. Appropriate feedback in CALL teacher training programs may enhance teachers ' self-evaluation and technology skills (Al-Furaih, 2017). The first theme, namely noticing problems, received much attention from the participants (n = 47). One participant highlighted its usefulness to addressing issues: ' Feedback from my professor and other groups helped me to identify and deal with some issues on our VE. ' Respondents further noted that the benefit of continuous feedback throughout the project led to improved output in the final stage (n = 19) as shared by one student in Extract 19.

## Extract 19

We used some comments from my professor, like how to make it more interactive, to improve our final product. Feedback from other groups was also highly useful to improve our VE for micro-teaching. (#52, Reflection)

Research shows that noting and coping with challenges are important parts of enacting teacher agency (Ashton, 2022; Chen, 2022; Priestley et al., 2015). Thus, this finding underscores the important role of a continuous feedback system in enhancing their technological knowledge in terms of noticing ICT affordances.

## 5. Discussion

Given that VEs affordances have been less explored in teacher education contexts, the study provided diverse VE affordances for language teaching identified  by Korean EFL pre-service teachers by using multiple data sources. For RQ1, which addressed VE affordances identified in the SQD-based VE development project, two affordances gained the most attention from the participants: communication (n = 107) and engaging learning environments (n = 104). While authentic communication based on social presence was noted in previous studies on VE affordances (e.g., Bahari, 2022; Jauregi et al., 2011; Mantziou et al., 2018; Warburton, 2009), communication-control and ubiquitous communication were new themes as the unique ways to boost L2 learners ' verbal communication  without  spatial  and  temporal  constraints.  Regarding  engaging  learning  environments,  experiential,  collaborative,  and game-based learnings were identified to serve as opportunities to increase engagement and immersion in authentic and stable language learning (Andreas et al., 2010; Gamage et al., 2011; Mantziou et al., 2018). This finding corresponds to current research on the benefits of VEs in providing diverse engaging strategies of language teaching, such as collaboration, role-plays, games, and problem-based tasks (Chen, 2018; Xu et al., 2020). The other two categories are more relevant to the design-based active learning perspective (Jeong, 2017; Lan et al., 2018): constructability (n = 69) and multimodal sharing (n = 63). The former afforded pre-service teachers ' enhanced technological awareness of how to design and customize authentic virtual spaces for L2 students ' needs, whereas the latter supported student interaction with their peers and learning materials both during and after class (Al-Furaih, 2017; Mantziou et al., 2018).

The findings further showed how those affordances were noticed and used by the participants. The SQD-strategies were used as the integrated model that encouraged EFL pre-service teachers to enact their agency in noticing and using VE affordances; the study linked the SQD model to the operation of agency and ICT affordance. Unlike previous research that simply reported VE affordances (e.g., Mantziou et al., 2018; Reiso ˘ glu et al., 2017), this study focused on the process by which student teachers learned how to design and develop their own virtual classrooms for L2 teaching, considering their stance as both learners and teachers. In other words, it J. Jeon et al.

described how this process of VE development benefited their noticing of VE affordances. Current studies on the design-based ICT development also argued for the positive outcome of such trainee-centered, active learning on enhanced technological awareness (e.g., Al-Furaih, 2017; Nami, 2022). This study advanced our understanding of the potential benefits of this hands-on ICT development project in promoting student teachers ' knowledge of ICT affordances in the SQD-based VE design context. To be more specific, drawing on the ecological perspective on the association between ICT affordance and teacher agency (Chen, 2022; White et al., 2021), it provided an description of how the seven SQD strategies helped EFL pre-service teachers enact their agency in noticing VE affordances for English language teaching in the Korean context. The qualitative findings indicate that the SQD strategies in the active learning environment  may  be  linked  to  their  exerted  agency  in  terms  of  CLT-based  teaching  goals  (forethoughts  with  goal-setting),  a trainee-centered design approach (self-regulation and self-efficacy), collaborative work on VE development (collective agency), and reflections on VE design and use (reflective analysis).

As for RQ2, the study investigated the perceived benefits of the SQD-strategies in noticing and utilizing VE affordances for EFL teaching. While previous studies used the SQD model to evaluate teacher-training programs (As ¸ık et al., 2020; Tondeur et al., 2012, 2016, 2020), this study adopted a qualitative-dominant mixed research method to take a close look at the participants ' in-depth voices. First, the descriptive analysis of survey data showed that while most of the strategies gained highly positive evaluations, alignment between theory and practice (Mean = 5.15; SD = 0.76) and modeling technology use (Mean = 5.21, SD = 1.02) displayed relatively lower rates. This finding indicates that a teacher-centered and theory-based view alone is not enough for a teacher education program particularly when the program adopts project-based tasks in which student teachers can lead their own learning. Therefore, the systematic approach of integrating student-led activities with instructor support is necessary for CALL teacher training programs. Put it another way, ' the systematic interplay of teacher- and student-centered approaches can be productive ' for teachers ' professional development (Nami, 2022, p. 120).

As shown in Appendix A, previous qualitative studies that adopted SQD strategies tested only some of them for teacher training (e. g., Campbell et al., 2020; Fathi &amp; Ebadi, 2020; Jeong, 2017). In comparison, the present study used all of the SQD strategies as an integrative model to explore in what ways the strategies benefited EFL pre-service teachers ' awareness of VE affordance for language teaching. Thus, the qualitative findings displayed underlying reasons behind the survey results: the first two strategies alone did not seem to fully satisfy the participants ' noticing VE affordances. For them, some abstract concepts were not easily realized in VE projects, and the instructor ' s provision of model VEs served as an initial reference only, not fully satisfying their active learning. On the one hand, this finding suggests the need for active ' communication between the instructor and pre-service teachers ' to enhance their knowledge  about  ICT  affordances  (Al-Furaih,  2017,  p.  3200).  This  interpretation  is  echoed  by  the  most  attention  attracted  to continuous feedback, which was perceived as useful to noticing problems and improved output. On the other hand, pre-service teachers ' active  engagement in technology development may enhance their self-efficacy, autonomy and motivation for noticing and using ICT affordances for language teaching (Kuru G ¨ onen, 2019; Tseng et al., 2019).

The other two strategies, namely collaborative work and actual technology use, provided constructive learning opportunities for the participants. Research shows the combined effect of the two strategies on promoting pre-service teachers ' technological, content knowledge (Jeong, 2017; Ma et al., 2020). Jeong (2017, p. 503) argued that the collaborative work on ICT development project for micro-teaching promoted EFL pre-service teachers ' confidence in ' their understanding of pedagogical purposes of educational technology. ' The qualitative findings of the study advanced our understanding of how these two strategies may facilitate EFL pre-service teachers ' attention to technology affordances in a process-based ICT development project. For example, some interviewees reported that  collaboration  provided  the  scaffolding  effect  on  guiding  pre-service  teachers,  who  were  yet  to  learn  using  new  tools,  into developing technological knowledge on their own by identifying, choosing, and exploiting ICT affordances for teaching practices.

The findings provide both academic and educational implications. First, teacher education programs may consider the affordance perspective to enhance student teachers ' attention to pedagogical benefits of ICT tools in diverse teaching contexts. Nami (2022) insisted that noticing ICT affordances is one of the core knowledge schemes in teacher training programs. Second, to facilitate their attention  to  ICT  affordances,  the  integrated  approach  to  student-centered  activities  is  necessary  (Tondeur  et  al.,  2016,  2020). Congruent  with  previous  studies  supporting  active  teacher-student  and  student-student  communication  in  CALL-based  teacher training (Al-Furaih, 2017; Campbell et al., 2020), this study suggests the SQD-strategies as the integrated model that includes both top-down (e.g., guidance of theory and instructors ' modeling and feedback) and bottom-up (e.g., collaborative work in technology design  and  use  for  teaching)  approaches.  In  particular,  the  authors  put  forward  the  design-based  active  learning  project  with continuous feedback as an authentic strategy to usher pre-service teachers to new technologies. Given these student teachers ' lack of ICT use experiences for teaching, it is important to offer such an integrated ICT-affordance learning environment in which they can lead their own learning based on their instructor ' s and peers ' scaffolding (Jeong, 2017; van Lier, 2008). Finally, based on the findings, the study suggested a potential interplay among the SQD-strategies, agency enactment, and affordance perception in a pre-service teacher training in a descriptive way.

## 6. Conclusion and limitations

This exploratory study illustrated how the SQD-strategies as an integrated model facilitated EFL pre-service teachers ' noticing and adopting of VE affordances by activating their teacher agency. The findings first suggested the diverse affordances of the customizable VE platform for EFL teaching, which emerged from the VE development project. The study further noted that the seven SQD strategies were recognized as beneficial to the participants ' affordance noticing and utilizing in the project. Based on the findings, the authors suggested that EFL pre-service teacher training programs adopt the SQD-strategies that integrate both top-down and bottom-up approaches to enhance their awareness of ICT affordances.

J. Jeon et al.

As with all studies, a few limitations should be acknowledged in this study. First, since the study adopted a descriptive approach based on the qualitative-dominant mixed research method, its results cannot be fully generalized to other contexts. Thus, further studies should use an inferential approach to validate the findings by using either a control group without the SQD strategies for comparison or an advanced statistical tool such as multivariate analysis. Second, although the qualitative data were coded by the experts of qualitative research, some coding biases could be included in analysis. To compensate for this limitation, future studies can use qualitative data analysis software, such as NVivo or MAXQDA, for systematic analysis (Bazeley, 2013).
