# Generative AI for English for academic purposes (EAP) materials development: A systematic review of affordances, challenges, and practical implications

# Abstract

The integration of generative AI (GenAI) into English for academic purposes (EAP) materials development has attracted significant attention due to its versatile capabilities. However, previous reviews rarely focused on teacher-led materials creation, a crucial aspect for understanding the practical application of GenAI to EAP. Therefore, this systematic review examines 18 empirical studies published between January 2023 and April 2026, focusing on research design, affordances, challenges, and implications regarding GenAI integration into EAP materials development. Findings show that studies employed both mixed-methods and qualitative approaches mainly relying on self-reported data. ChatGPT dominated tool usage, while efficiency and personalization emerged as primary affordances. Accuracy concerns (e.g., hallucinations), ethical challenges (e.g., academic integrity), and pedagogical limitations (e.g., over-reliance) represented the most significant constraints. GenAI served diverse roles including content generator, feedback provider, text adapter, and co-creator; however, technological challenges, including prompt dependency and output quality, and educational challenges, such as verification workload and skill gaps, complicated GenAI integration. The review suggests that future research should diversify contexts, employ longitudinal designs, and develop institutional support for teacher professional development in AI literacy. For practitioners, findings indicate the need for developed prompting strategies, systematic verification protocols, and balanced human-AI collaboration frameworks.

**Keywords**: generative AI (GenAI), systematic review, EAP materials development, English for academic purposes

---

# 1. Introduction

The launch of ChatGPT in late 2022 has marked a watershed moment in language education, catalyzing unprecedented interest in generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) applications across diverse learning contexts (Chiu, 2023; Kohnke et al., 2023). Unlike earlier AI chatbots that relied on predefined dialogue scenarios and limited corpora, GenAI tools leveraging large language models (LLMs)—such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Copilot—enable natural, contextually rich interactions that have fundamentally transformed how language teaching and learning are conceptualized (Huang et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2023; Schneider, 2024). Within the broader field of language education, English for Academic Purposes (EAP) has emerged as a particularly significant domain for GenAI integration, given its focus on preparing learners for discipline-specific academic communication through authentic materials, targeted instruction, and assessment aligned with disciplinary conventions (Hyland, 2006; Hyland & Shaw, 2016).

The rapid proliferation of GenAI in language education has prompted scholarly attention to its pedagogical affordances and challenges. Recent systematic reviews have documented the roles of GenAI in language learning, including feedback provider, learning tutor, conversation partner, and cognitive stimulator (Law, 2024; Lai & Lee, 2024; Li et al., 2024; Teng, 2024b; Yang & Li, 2024). Empirical studies have demonstrated that GenAI can support language skill development through personalized feedback (Karataş et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2025; Tai & Chen, 2024), foster learner autonomy and reduce anxiety (Ghafouri, 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), and accommodate different proficiency levels and individual differences. However, these benefits are accompanied by concerns regarding academic integrity, content accuracy, feedback quality, data security, and over-reliance (Abdelhalim, 2024; Long, 2024; Tseng & Lin, 2024; Zou & Huang, 2023).

Within EAP specifically, the integration of GenAI has attracted research attention, though systematic syntheses remain limited. Recent reviews have examined the impact of GenAI on academic writing instruction in higher education (Aljuaid, 2024), mapped GenAI applications across writing stages in academic contexts (Gabay et al., 2025), and surveyed emerging trends across the EAP field (Pomat, 2025). These reviews collectively indicate that GenAI is being adopted across multiple dimensions of EAP practice, including feedback provision, writing support, reading comprehension scaffolding, and assessment. However, they also reveal that existing syntheses have predominantly focused on student-facing applications (how learners use GenAI for writing, revision, and language practice) rather than pedagogical activities led by instructors.

Among the dimensions of EAP practice, materials development represents a core practitioner activity that directly shapes instructional quality and learner outcomes (Tomlinson, 2011; Tomlinson & Masuhara, 2004). EAP materials development encompasses the creation, adaptation, and evaluation of discipline-specific learning resources, including reading texts, writing prompts, vocabulary exercises, speaking activities, and assessment instruments (Basturkmen, 2010; Flowerdew & Peacock, 2001). This work demands considerable time investment, disciplinary knowledge, and pedagogical expertise, particularly when developing authentic materials that reflect disciplinary discourse conventions (Hyland, 2006). The capacity of GenAI for rapid text generation, task design, and customization offers potential to support materials development led by teachers, yet this application has received limited systematic attention in the literature.

Recent conceptual and empirical work has begun to explore the role of GenAI in EAP materials development. Scholars have documented how EAP practitioners use GenAI to generate reading passages, create writing prompts, design vocabulary exercises, develop rubrics, and adapt materials for different proficiency levels (Cheung & Crosthwaite, 2025; Eljack et al., 2025; Hu, 2026; Wardat & Akour, 2025; Xin, 2024; Zhu et al., 2025). These studies suggest that GenAI can offer affordances for materials development, including efficiency gains, personalization capabilities, and expanded access to discipline-specific content. However, they also raise concerns about accuracy, authenticity, pedagogical appropriateness, and the risk of over-reliance on AI-generated materials without adequate human oversight (Morgana & Poli, 2026; Zhang & Wang, 2025).

Despite this emerging body of empirical work, no systematic review has specifically examined the role of GenAI in EAP materials development. Existing reviews have either addressed academic writing instruction broadly without focusing on materials development (Aljuaid, 2024; Gabay et al., 2025), examined technology integration as one of many EAP research themes without GenAI-specific analysis (Pomat, 2025), or focused on general language learning contexts rather than EAP specifically (Law, 2024; Lai & Lee, 2024; Li et al., 2024; Teng, 2024b; Yang & Li, 2024). This gap is particularly problematic given that materials development represents a fundamental EAP practitioner activity that directly impacts instructional quality, teacher workload, and learner outcomes. Without systematic evidence on how GenAI is being used for EAP materials development, what affordances it offers, what challenges practitioners encounter, and what implications emerge for practice and policy, EAP practitioners lack an evidence base to inform their pedagogical decisions and institutional leaders lack guidance for developing appropriate support structures.

Therefore, this systematic review addresses this gap by examining empirical studies on GenAI use in EAP materials development. Specifically, we review 18 empirical studies published between January 2023 and April 2026 to synthesize current evidence on research designs, affordances, challenges, and implications for EAP practitioners. This review aims to provide an understanding of how GenAI is currently being utilized in EAP materials development, what benefits and challenges emerge from practice, and what implications these findings hold for educators, institutions, and future research agendas.

# 2. Literature Review

## 2.1. Affordances and challenges of GenAI for EAP instruction

The emergence of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI), particularly large language models like ChatGPT, has fundamentally transformed the landscape of English language instruction in higher education contexts (Crosthwaite, 2025; Gabay et al., 2025). This technological revolution presents a dual-edged sword for English for Academic Purposes (EAP) and English as a Foreign Language (EFL) pedagogy, offering unprecedented opportunities for personalized learning while simultaneously raising critical concerns about academic integrity and skill development (Aljuaid, 2024; Pomat, 2025). As GenAI tools become increasingly sophisticated and accessible, EAP practitioners face the urgent challenge of developing balanced, evidence-based frameworks that leverage AI affordances while mitigating potential risks to learner autonomy and critical thinking (Cong-Lem, 2026; Zhang & Liu, 2026). Understanding this complex duality is essential for informing pedagogical decisions, institutional policies, and assessment practices in the AI era (Mekheimer, 2025).

Research consistently demonstrates that GenAI offers significant pedagogical affordances for EAP instruction, particularly in providing immediate, individualized feedback that enhances learner autonomy and writing development (Crosthwaite, 2025). Studies reveal that AI-generated feedback is perceived by EFL learners as more relevant to their specific needs compared to traditional teacher feedback, offering balanced coverage across content, organization, and language domains (Li et al., 2024; Aljasser, 2025). The immediacy and accessibility of GenAI feedback enable students to engage in iterative revision processes, with empirical evidence showing that students using AI-assisted feedback tools demonstrate significantly higher revision frequency and engagement compared to those receiving only traditional instructor feedback (Mekheimer, 2025). Moreover, GenAI serves as an effective writing collaborator, providing scaffolding in content development, vocabulary enhancement, and structural organization while allowing students to maintain agency over their writing process (Huang & Wang, 2025). The technology's capacity to provide continuous, individualized support reduces writing anxiety and fosters positive motivation, particularly benefiting L2 writers who require additional language support (Bai & Wei, 2024; Zhang & Liu, 2026). Recent design-based research has identified effective dialogic feedback structures where ChatGPT functions adaptively as both peer collaborator during brainstorming and assessor during revision stages, demonstrating the potential for sophisticated pedagogical integration (Zhang & Liu, 2026).

Despite its affordances, GenAI integration in EAP instruction raises substantial ethical and cognitive concerns that require careful consideration (Crosthwaite, 2025). Academic integrity represents the most prominent challenge, as GenAI's capacity to generate human-like text blurs boundaries between student-authored work and machine-generated content, complicating assessment validity and raising questions about intellectual authorship (Cong-Lem, 2026; Gabay et al., 2025). The phenomenon of AI hallucination—where GenAI produces factually incorrect information, fabricated citations, and misleading content—poses significant risks to learner knowledge acquisition and academic credibility (Crosthwaite, 2025; Aljuaid, 2024). Cognitive dependency emerges as another critical concern, with evidence suggesting that over-reliance on AI feedback may hinder the development of independent critical thinking skills, reduce creativity, and limit students' ability to develop their unique academic voice (Cong-Lem, 2026; Tsai et al., 2024). Research indicates that while AI-assisted revisions lead to significantly higher writing scores, particularly in vocabulary and grammar dimensions, this improvement may not accurately reflect learners' actual writing competence, raising fairness concerns in assessment contexts (Tsai et al., 2024). Furthermore, the risk of surface-level engagement with AI suggestions, without deep critical evaluation, may compromise the development of higher-order thinking skills essential for academic success (Mekheimer, 2025).

The rapid proliferation of GenAI tools has exposed a significant gap between technology adoption and the critical AI literacy required by both students and instructors for effective integration (Crosthwaite, 2025; Pomat, 2025). Empirical studies reveal that without structured guidance and AI literacy training, students struggle to formulate effective prompts, critically evaluate AI output, and strategically integrate AI assistance into their writing processes (Zhang & Liu, 2026; Bai & Wei, 2024). The challenge extends beyond individual competency to institutional support structures, as EAP programs require comprehensive professional development, clear policy frameworks, and process-based assessment approaches to mitigate AI-related risks while maximizing pedagogical benefits (Cong-Lem, 2026; Aljuaid, 2024). Research emphasizes the necessity of balanced assessment methods that integrate both AI feedback and human oversight, with teacher-led interviews emerging as a promising dialogic accountability mechanism that fosters ethical engagement and student reflection on AI use (Cong-Lem, 2026). Effective pedagogical integration requires moving beyond detection-based surveillance toward development-oriented approaches that cultivate students' critical AI literacy, including understanding benefits and limitations, ethical use, prompt engineering, and critical evaluation of AI-generated content (Crosthwaite, 2025; Zhang & Liu, 2026). Institutional responses must address the need for discipline-specific guidance, graduated mediation strategies, and mobilization of learners' full tool repertoires to ensure GenAI serves as a complement to, rather than replacement for, human instruction (Zhang & Liu, 2026; Mekheimer, 2025).

## 2.2. The use of GenAI for EAP material development

The integration of GenAI into EAP materials development has emerged as a critical research focus, reflecting the technology's transformative potential for supporting teacher-led creation of discipline-specific learning resources (Zhu et al., 2025; Hu, 2026). As EAP instruction increasingly demands authentic, contextually relevant materials that address specific disciplinary discourse conventions, GenAI offers unprecedented efficiency in generating reading texts, writing prompts, vocabulary exercises, and assessment instruments tailored to learners' needs (Zhang & Wang, 2025; Morgana & Poli, 2026). This capacity for rapid, customizable material generation is particularly valuable for EAP practitioners who often face time constraints and limited access to discipline-specific expertise when developing course materials (Eljack et al., 2025; Cheung & Crosthwaite, 2025). The following sections examine three prominent applications of GenAI in EAP materials development: automated text generation for discipline-specific reading materials, AI-assisted task and exercise design, and GenAI-supported assessment instrument creation.

GenAI demonstrates significant affordances for generating authentic, discipline-specific reading materials that align with EAP learners' academic needs. Recent studies have explored the use of ChatGPT and similar tools to create simplified academic texts, generate supplementary reading passages on specialized topics, and adapt authentic materials to appropriate proficiency levels (Fan et al., 2024; Xin, 2024). Research by Zhu et al. (2025) found that EAP instructors using GenAI for text generation reported substantial time savings while maintaining content quality and disciplinary accuracy, though they emphasized the necessity of human review for verifying factual accuracy and rhetorical appropriateness. Similarly, Hu (2026) documented how GenAI-enabled text adaptation allowed EAP teachers to customize reading materials for diverse proficiency levels within the same course, addressing a longstanding challenge in heterogeneous EAP classrooms. However, scholars caution that AI-generated texts may lack the authentic disciplinary discourse features that EAP learners need to encounter, potentially limiting their preparation for genuine academic reading tasks (Morgana & Poli, 2026).

GenAI has proven valuable for designing varied and pedagogically sound EAP tasks and exercises that target specific language and academic skills. Empirical investigations reveal that EAP practitioners leverage GenAI to generate writing prompts aligned with disciplinary genres, create vocabulary exercises featuring academic word lists in context, and design speaking activities that simulate academic communication scenarios (Al-Obaydi et al., 2025; Wardat & Akour, 2025). A study by Ngo and Hastie (2025) demonstrated that task design with GenAI enabled instructors to produce differentiated exercises addressing varied learner needs more efficiently than traditional manual methods, while maintaining pedagogical coherence and learning objective alignment. Research by Tseng and Lin (2024) further highlighted the capacity of GenAI to generate corpus-informed exercises that expose learners to authentic disciplinary language patterns, though the authors noted that instructor expertise remains essential for selecting appropriate linguistic features and ensuring pedagogical progression. The ability of the technology to rapidly produce multiple task variants also supports formative assessment practices, allowing instructors to provide learners with extensive practice opportunities without proportional increases in preparation time (ElEbyary & Ramy Shabara, 2024).

Emerging research examines the role of GenAI in developing EAP assessment instruments, including rubrics, test items, and performance evaluation criteria. Studies indicate that GenAI can assist in creating analytic rubrics for discipline-specific writing tasks, generating multiple-choice questions for reading comprehension assessment, and designing peer evaluation frameworks for collaborative projects (Mekheimer, 2025; Ngo & Fians, 2026). Cheung and Crosthwaite (2025) found that EAP instructors who used GenAI for rubric development produced more detailed and criterion-referenced assessment tools compared to those working without AI assistance, though human judgment remained important for ensuring validity and reliability. However, scholars raise important concerns about over-reliance on AI-generated assessments, noting that such instruments may not adequately capture the nuanced disciplinary expectations and higher-order thinking skills that EAP instruction aims to develop (Zhang & Wang, 2025). The literature emphasizes that GenAI should serve as a supplementary tool in assessment development, while instructors maintain primary responsibility for ensuring alignment with learning outcomes and disciplinary standards (Williyan et al., 2024; Mzwri & Turcsányi-Szabo, 2025).

## 2.3. Previous systematic reviews on GenAI and EAP research

The rapid proliferation of GenAI in educational contexts has prompted several systematic reviews examining the technology's implications for language teaching and learning. These reviews provide syntheses of empirical evidence, methodological approaches, and emerging themes that inform the focus on GenAI in EAP materials development. This section examines three recent systematic reviews that address overlapping but distinct aspects of AI integration in language education, highlighting their contributions and limitations.

Aljuaid's (2024) systematic review examined 43 studies on the impact of AI tools on academic writing instruction in higher education, providing insights into both benefits and limitations from multiple stakeholder perspectives. Using PRISMA guidelines, the review identified five major themes: traditional writing courses' pedagogical value, AI efficiency and automation benefits, AI limitations in critical thinking development, integrated approach advantages, and risks of sole AI reliance. The review's strength lies in its balanced synthesis of student and teacher perceptions, revealing that while students favor AI convenience, teachers express concern about over-reliance and skill atrophy. However, Aljuaid's (2024) review focused broadly on academic writing instruction across all disciplines without specifically addressing EAP contexts or materials development practices, limiting its applicability to discipline-specific language instruction.

Gabay et al. (2025) conducted a scoping review of 25 empirical studies examining GenAI applications in academic writing within higher education, mapping current tool usage patterns, benefits, and challenges across writing stages. The review framework identified seven functional domains of GenAI use, from pre-writing idea generation to revision and language support, with emphasis on EAP and L2 writing contexts. Gabay et al.'s (2025) review provides insights into how GenAI supports individual writing processes, but it primarily examined student-facing applications rather than materials development activities led by instructors. The review's focus on learner experiences and pedagogical implications, though important, does not address how EAP practitioners leverage GenAI for creating instructional resources, representing a gap in the literature.

Pomat's (2025) systematic literature review of 71 EAP research articles from 2020-2024 examined emerging trends across the EAP field, including technology integration as one of several thematic categories. The review identified teacher development, curriculum innovation, and assessment practices as dominant research focuses, with technology integration receiving comparatively less attention. Pomat (2025) noted the increasing presence of AI-related research in EAP journals, but the review did not specifically synthesize GenAI-focused studies or examine materials development as a distinct research domain. The review's broad scope across all EAP research themes limited its depth of analysis regarding AI-specific pedagogical applications and challenges.

Despite these valuable contributions, significant gaps remain in the systematic review literature regarding GenAI and EAP research. First, comprehensive reviews specifically focused on "GenAI and EAP research" remain scarce, with most existing reviews addressing broader academic writing contexts or general language learning applications rather than discipline-specific language instruction (Aljuaid, 2024; Gabay et al., 2025). Second, existing reviews rarely focus specifically on instructor-centered empirical studies examining how EAP practitioners integrate GenAI into their pedagogical practices, particularly materials development activities. The majority of reviewed studies examined student experiences, perceptions, and learning outcomes, with comparatively limited attention to teacher agency, decision-making, and professional development needs. Third, and most critically, no systematic review has specifically examined GenAI's role in EAP materials development, despite the prominence of this practice in EAP instruction and the technology's demonstrated capacity for text generation, task design, and assessment creation. This gap is particularly problematic given that materials development represents a core EAP practitioner activity that directly impacts instructional quality and learner outcomes. Therefore, this systematic review addresses these gaps by examining empirical studies on GenAI use in EAP materials development, focusing on research designs, affordances, challenges, and implications for EAP practitioners. Four research questions guide this review. 

(1) What are the current research trends and methodological characteristics in GenAI-assisted EAP materials development? 

(2) What affordances does GenAI offer for developing EAP materials? 

(3) What challenges do EAP practitioners encounter when integrating GenAI into materials development?

(4) What are the pedagogical implications for EAP practitioners and future research?

# 3. Methods

## 3.1. Data collection process

We used the PRISMA guidelines to collect data for the research (Moher et al., 2009; Page et al., 2021). Drawing on previous studies on AI chatbots and GenAI in EAP contexts, we used Web of Science and SCOPUS to identify peer-reviewed publications. To identify search terms relevant to the research, we reviewed cited peer-reviewed journals across education fields, including language education, computer-assisted language learning, English for Academic Purposes, general education, and educational technology.

We determined search keywords on GenAI ("Generative AI" OR "Generative artificial intelligence" OR "ChatGPT" OR "GPT" OR "Large language model" OR "Bing" OR "Copilot" OR "Claude" OR "Gemini" OR "Bard") and EAP materials development ("English for Academic Purposes" OR "EAP" OR "Academic English" OR "English in higher education" OR "materials development" OR "teaching materials" OR "lesson plan" OR "assessment" OR "feedback").

Our search was limited to research articles published between 1 January 2023 and 15 April 2026 (see Figure 1), considering the public release of ChatGPT in late 2022. Using "title", "abstract", and "keywords" as filtering criteria, we initially found 1,071 articles written in English from the two databases: Web of Science (n = 394) and SCOPUS (n = 677). After excluding duplicates (n = 287), we included 784 studies for title and abstract screening.

During the title and abstract screening stage, we excluded 711 studies that did not meet the inclusion criteria for the following reasons: not research articles (e.g., review, forum, letter, commentary, editorial), not including EAP context (English for Academic Purposes or English in higher education), not about specific GenAI tool(s), and not about materials development. As a result, 73 full-text articles were assessed for eligibility.

During the full-text screening stage, we excluded 55 articles for the following reasons: not focusing on EAP, not relevant to GenAI, not focusing on EAP materials development, and not relevant to teacher roles. Accordingly, a total of 18 studies were included in the final set for data analysis.

For this review, studies were included if they met all of the following criteria: (a) empirical study published as a peer-reviewed journal article written in English; (b) conducted in EAP contexts, including English for Academic Purposes, English in higher education, EFL/ESL in academic contexts, or academic English teaching/learning; (c) used specific GenAI tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, Bard, or other LLM-based generative AI tools; and (d) focused on materials development, including teaching resources, activities, assessment materials, or feedback created, adapted, or curated by teachers or researchers using GenAI for pedagogical purposes.

Studies were excluded if they were: (a) non-empirical publications such as review articles, editorials, commentaries, conference papers, or book chapters; (b) not focused on EAP or academic English contexts, including K-12 only settings without higher education components; (c) not relevant to GenAI, including traditional AI tools like grammar checkers without generation capabilities or AI detection tools; (d) not focused on materials development, including student self-directed GenAI use without teacher intervention or GenAI outputs not curated or evaluated as formal teaching resources; or (e) focused only on teacher training without EAP materials development components.

For this review, EAP materials development was defined as teaching resources, activities, assessment materials, and feedback created by teachers or researchers using GenAI for pedagogical purposes, limited to what is intentionally prepared or curated by teachers or researchers within the study. This definition includes materials that are generated, adapted, or evaluated by teachers or researchers for use in EAP classrooms, but excludes student self-directed use of GenAI without teacher intervention.

## 3.2. Data analysis

To address the research questions, we adopted a hybrid thematic analysis combining deductive and inductive methods (Fereday & Muir-Cochrane, 2006). First, we developed a coding scheme with various research dimensions and categories based on previous reviews of AI chatbots and GenAI in EAP contexts (Table 1). For RQ1 (research trends), we incorporated categories including location, educational level, sample size and language proficiency, research method, data source, intervention duration, and specific GenAI tool, drawing on coding schemes from previous systematic reviews (Huang et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2023; Law, 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Mai et al., 2024). For RQ2 (affordances), we examined the roles of GenAI in materials development and the benefits reported across studies, adapting categories from prior reviews of AI in language education (Lee et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024; Yang & Li, 2024). For RQ3 (challenges), we adopted challenges associated with GenAI use from review articles and identified additional challenges specific to EAP materials development (Mai et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024). For RQ4 (implications), we categorized research and pedagogical implications reported in the reviewed studies.

Next, we conducted a thematic analysis using a hybrid approach that combined deductive and inductive coding. We first applied the coding scheme from previous review studies to the 18 selected studies. While our initial categories covered most data, we identified gaps where important themes were missing. To address these gaps, we introduced an inductive phase using iterative cycles of open coding and constant comparison. Two authors independently reviewed the datasets, assigning initial codes to content that did not fit the existing scheme. We refined and validated these emerging codes through an iterative process, merging overlapping codes and developing clear operational definitions. The refined codes were then applied to the entire dataset to ensure analysis consistency. For validation, two researchers coded overlapping data subsets. The inter-coder reliability for the final coding results, calculated using Cohen's kappa, showed a high agreement level of 0.85. Any remaining discrepancies were resolved through discussion until consensus was reached on the final codes, which are reflected in our findings.

# 4. Results

## 4.1. RQ1: Research trends and methodological characteristics

### 4.1.1. Geographical distribution

The geographical distribution of the 18 reviewed studies reveals a diverse but regionally concentrated research landscape (Table 2). Asian contexts dominated the sample (n = 10, 55.6%), with China representing the largest single national context (n = 6, including mainland China, Hong Kong, and Taiwan). This concentration aligns with broader patterns in GenAI-language education research, where Asian higher education institutions have been at the forefront of empirical investigation (Lai & Lee, 2024; Li et al., 2024). Middle Eastern contexts comprised four studies (Egypt n = 2, Sudan n = 1, Jordan n = 1), reflecting growing interest in GenAI applications for EAP in regions where English serves as a medium of instruction in higher education. European contexts were represented by two studies from the United Kingdom (Ngo & Fians, 2026; Ngo & Hastie, 2025), both examining EAP practitioners' use of GenAI in pre-sessional and in-sessional programs. One study did not specify location (Morgana & Poli, 2026). The predominance of Asian and Middle Eastern contexts suggests that GenAI adoption in EAP materials development may be particularly salient in settings where English functions as an additional language for academic purposes, though this concentration also limits generalizability to other educational contexts.

### 4.1.2. Educational contexts and participant characteristics

All 18 studies (100%) focused exclusively on higher education contexts, including university undergraduates, postgraduate students, and university-level EAP instructors. This complete concentration in tertiary education mirrors findings from systematic reviews on GenAI in language learning more broadly (Law, 2024; Lee et al., 2025; Teng, 2024b), likely reflecting both the accessibility of university settings for researchers and the pressure that GenAI has placed on tertiary EAP instruction following the public release of ChatGPT. No studies examined EAP materials development in secondary education, pre-sessional pathway colleges outside universities, or workplace English contexts, representing a gap in the literature.

Sample sizes varied considerably across studies, ranging from small-scale qualitative inquiries to large mixed-methods investigations. Five studies (27.8%) employed small samples (≤ 10 participants), typically using qualitative designs to examine teacher experiences in depth (e.g., Xin, 2024, n = 3 teachers; Williyan et al., 2024, n = 5 teachers; Huang & Wang, 2025, n = 9 students). Four studies (22.2%) used medium samples (11-30 participants), including Cheung and Crosthwaite's (2025) mixed-methods study of 19 EAP students and Zhu et al.'s (2025) quasi-experimental design with 24 participants. Eight studies (44.4%) employed large samples (31-100 participants), with Çelik et al. (2024) recruiting 105 Turkish EFL learners for their quantitative study and Hu (2026) involving 72 Taiwanese undergraduates. Only one study (5.6%) used an extra-large sample (Wardat & Akour, 2025, n = 87 Jordanian EFL teachers). This distribution indicates a field balancing between exploratory qualitative work and larger-scale outcome investigations.

Language proficiency levels were specified in nine studies (50%), with intermediate proficiency (CEFR B1-B2) being most common (n = 8). Only one study focused on advanced learners (Huang & Wang, 2025, C1-C2). Nine studies did not specify proficiency levels, particularly those focusing on teacher participants rather than learners. This concentration on intermediate learners suggests that current evidence reflects GenAI application with learners who have sufficient language foundation to engage with AI-generated materials critically, though this leaves unanswered questions about GenAI's appropriateness for beginner or advanced EAP contexts.

### 4.1.3. Research methods and data sources

The reviewed studies employed methodological approaches, with mixed-methods designs being most prevalent (n = 9, 50%), followed by qualitative approaches (n = 8, 44.4%) (Table 3). Only one study (5.6%) used a purely quantitative design (Çelik et al., 2024). This methodological distribution indicates a field in its formative stages. Mixed-methods studies typically combined quantitative measures (e.g., writing tests, reading comprehension assessments, questionnaires) with qualitative data (e.g., interviews, reflective writing, learning artifacts), enabling researchers to capture both outcomes and processes of GenAI integration (e.g., Hu, 2026; Mekheimer, 2025; Zhang & Wang, 2025). Qualitative studies predominantly employed interviews, document analysis of AI-generated materials, and classroom observations to examine teacher decision-making and student experiences (e.g., Ngo & Fians, 2026; Xin, 2024; Tseng & Lin, 2024).

Data collection methods varied substantially across studies, with interviews being the most prevalent data source (n = 14, 77.8%). Interviews were conducted with both teachers (examining their materials development practices, decision-making processes, and perceptions of AI-generated materials) and students (exploring their experiences with and responses to AI-generated materials). Questionnaires and surveys appeared in seven studies (38.9%), typically measuring attitudes, self-efficacy, or perceived usefulness of GenAI tools. Test items, including writing assessments and reading comprehension tests, were used in seven studies (38.9%) to evaluate learning outcomes from AI-generated materials (e.g., Çelik et al., 2024; Hu, 2026; Mekheimer, 2025). Study logs or interaction records appeared in six studies (33.3%), capturing real-time data on how teachers and students engaged with GenAI tools (e.g., Cheung & Crosthwaite, 2025; Huang & Wang, 2025). Learning artifacts, such as student essays or teacher-created materials, were analyzed in five studies (27.8%), while reflection papers were used in only two studies (11.1%). The heavy reliance on self-reported data suggests potential for social desirability bias and indicates a need for more observational and behavioral data in future research.

### 4.1.4. Intervention duration and GenAI tools

Study durations varied considerably, with nearly half of the studies (n = 8, 44.4%) not specifying intervention duration. These studies typically focused on one-time materials development activities or retrospective evaluation of GenAI use rather than sustained integration over time. Among studies specifying duration, short-term interventions (≤ 10 weeks) were most common (n = 6, 33.3%), including Mekheimer's (2025) 8-week quasi-experimental study comparing Grammarly feedback with teacher feedback and Zhu et al.'s (2025) 10-week intervention with an AI-powered corpus platform. Only two studies (11.1%) employed long-term designs exceeding 16 weeks: Eljack et al. (2025) conducted a semester-long study in Sudanese EAP contexts, and Hu (2026) implemented a 17-week intervention in Taiwanese universities. One study (5.6%) used an intermediate-term design (11-16 weeks), and one study (5.6%) was a single-session intervention (Çelik et al., 2024). This distribution indicates that most evidence on GenAI for EAP materials development comes from short-term or unspecified-duration studies

ChatGPT dominated the GenAI tool landscape, appearing in 13 of 18 studies (72.2%) either exclusively or in combination with other tools. Nine studies (50%) used ChatGPT exclusively, reflecting the tool's market dominance and early mover advantage following its November 2022 public release. Four studies (22.2%) combined ChatGPT with other tools: Ngo and Fians (2026) examined EAP practitioners using ChatGPT alongside Copilot and Gemini; Ngo and Hastie (2025) investigated a broader toolkit including ChatGPT, Elicit, Consensus, Perplexity, and Quillbot; Wardat and Akour (2025) studied teachers using ChatGPT, ChatPDF, Grammarly, and Bard; and Williyan et al. (2024) documented Indonesian teachers combining ChatGPT with other unspecified AI tools. Four studies (22.2%) used custom or other tools exclusively: Mekheimer (2025) focused on Grammarly Premium; Zhu et al. (2025) developed a GPT-4-powered corpus platform; Zhang and Wang (2025) examined XIPU AI, an Azure-based custom system; and Xin (2024) studied ChatPDF for reading materials adaptation. One study (5.6%) did not specify the GenAI tool used (Fan et al., 2024). This distribution reflects both the dominance of ChatGPT and emerging diversification

### 4.1.5. Research focus categories

The 18 studies were classified into three research focus categories: Perception, Outcomes, and Development, with studies potentially coded in multiple categories when applicable (Table 2). Perception-focused studies (n = 8, 44.4%) examined attitudes, beliefs, and practices related to GenAI use in materials development. This category included investigations of teacher attitudes and beliefs (n = 2), teacher practices in materials creation (n = 3), student attitudes toward AI-generated materials (n = 2), and student practices when engaging with such materials (n = 1). For example, Zhang and Wang (2025) surveyed Chinese EAP students' perceptions of AI-generated feedback interpretation materials, and Fan et al. (2024) interviewed Hong Kong students about their experiences with AI-created reading texts.

Outcomes-focused studies (n = 8, 44.4%) investigated the effects of GenAI-generated materials on learning outcomes, including language skill development (n = 5), performance comparisons between AI-generated and human-created materials (n = 2), and affective outcomes such as motivation or anxiety reduction (n = 1). Notable examples include Hu's (2026) quasi-experimental study demonstrating that glocalized ChatGPT-generated materials improved Taiwanese students' reading comprehension and cultural awareness, and Mekheimer's (2025) finding that Grammarly-generated feedback led to higher writing scores compared to teacher feedback among Egyptian EFL postgraduates.

Development-focused studies (n = 8, 44.4%) examined the process of creating, adapting, or designing materials and tools with GenAI. This category included materials creation (n = 2), materials adaptation (n = 2), tool/system design (n = 2), and framework implementation (n = 2). Cheung and Crosthwaite (2025) developed CorpusChat, a corpus-informed GenAI platform for EAP materials, and Ngo and Hastie (2025) designed a framework for EAP practitioners integrating multiple GenAI tools into their materials development workflows. The equal distribution across these three categories suggests a field exploring what teachers and students think about GenAI materials, what effects these materials produce, and how such materials can be developed.

Accuracy concerns dominated the challenge landscape, with nine studies (50.0%) reporting hallucinations or factual errors requiring teacher verification. Hu (2026) emphasized that "teachers need to cautiously evaluate the responses by ChatGPT using their teaching knowledge and teaching experience," documenting instances where ChatGPT-generated reading comprehension questions contained factual inaccuracies. Xin (2024) documented how acceptance of ChatPDF output varied widely (10% to 62.5%) across three Chinese teachers, with rejection rates reflecting concerns about factual reliability and disciplinary accuracy. Ngo and Fians (2026) reported that UK EAP practitioners developed verification protocols, cross-checking AI-generated content against authoritative sources before classroom use. Ethical challenges were widespread, with seven studies (38.9%) citing academic integrity and plagiarism concerns. Tseng and Lin (2024) found that Taiwanese students reflected critically on "overreliance, authorship, and originality" when using ChatGPT for materials generation, expressing concern about whether AI-assisted work represented their own learning. Zhang and Wang (2025) noted Chinese student concerns about academic dishonesty when using GenAI to interpret teacher feedback, questioning whether such use constituted legitimate learning support. Huang and Wang (2025) documented how advanced Chinese EFL learners grappled with authorship ambiguity when co-creating materials with ChatGPT. Ngo and Hastie (2025) reported that UK EAP practitioners struggled with disclosure questions—whether and how to acknowledge AI contributions to course materials. Wardat and Akour (2025), Al-Obaydi et al. (2025), and Mekheimer (2025) similarly noted teacher and student concerns about academic integrity, particularly regarding assessment materials and the boundary between legitimate support and unfair advantage.

Over-reliance and dependency concerns appeared in six studies (33.3%), reflecting worries that GenAI use might undermine learner autonomy or teacher expertise. Tseng and Lin (2024) found that some Taiwanese students accepted ChatGPT output uncritically. Huang and Wang (2025) documented how advanced Chinese learners occasionally deferred to ChatGPT suggestions even when their own linguistic intuition suggested alternatives. Zhang and Wang (2025) reported that Chinese students using GenAI to interpret teacher feedback sometimes prioritized AI explanations over instructor guidance. Mekheimer (2025) and Al-Obaydi et al. (2025) similarly noted concerns about students accepting AI feedback without critical evaluation.

Technical challenges, particularly prompt dependency, were reported in six studies (33.3%). Xin (2024) found that successful GenAI use depended substantially on the pedagogical content knowledge of teachers and prompt-design ability, and there was marked variation in output quality across teachers who had different prompting expertise. Ngo and Fians (2026) documented how UK EAP practitioners employed both "detailed prompting" (providing extensive context and specifications) and "minimal dialogic prompting" (iterative refinement through conversation) strategies to work around GenAI limitations. Zhang and Wang (2025) reported that Chinese students struggled to formulate effective prompts for interpreting teacher feedback, often receiving generic or misaligned responses. Tseng and Lin (2024), Zhu et al. (2025), and Hu (2026) all noted that prompt engineering represented a learning curve for both teachers and students, requiring practice and institutional support.

Pedagogical limitations were noted in four studies (22.2%), including lack of pedagogical insight and limited higher-order thinking support. Fan et al. (2024) found that Hong Kong students valued GenAI for grammar correction, but they preferred teacher feedback for "precise, context-sensitive support" in disciplinary writing, noting that AI lacked understanding of assignment-specific expectations. Zhu et al. (2025) reported that their corpus platform improved language use and grammar, but evidence use and organization did not improve significantly, suggesting limitations in supporting higher-order writing skills. Huang and Wang (2025) documented how ChatGPT sometimes produced formulaic responses that failed to stimulate critical thinking or deep engagement with complex disciplinary concepts. Xin (2024) and Ngo and Fians (2026) similarly noted that GenAI outputs sometimes lacked pedagogical sophistication, requiring teacher adaptation to align with learning objectives.

Linguistic concerns, including over-simplification and unnatural outputs, appeared in four studies (22.2%). Huang and Wang (2025) documented how ChatGPT sometimes produced "formulaic responses" and "repetitive or fixed writing" patterns that failed to model the linguistic diversity of authentic academic discourse. ElEbyary and Shabara (2024) noted that ChatGPT tended to rewrite full sentences rather than provide targeted corrections, which may not optimally support lower-proficiency learners. Çelik et al. # 5. Discussion

This systematic review examined 18 empirical studies on GenAI use in EAP materials development, addressing four research questions: (1) current research trends and methodological characteristics, (2) affordances for developing EAP materials, (3) challenges practitioners encounter, and (4) pedagogical and institutional implications. This section synthesizes findings, discusses research gaps, and proposes directions for future research.

## 5.1. Key findings on GenAI for EAP materials development

The findings reveal a field in rapid evolution. Several themes emerged from our synthesis.

The exclusive focus on higher education contexts (100% of studies) reflects both the accessibility of university settings for researchers and the pressure that GenAI has placed on tertiary EAP instruction. This finding aligns with systematic reviews in language education, where higher education dominates AI-related research (Lee et al., 2025). However, this concentration limits understanding of how GenAI might support EAP materials development in other contexts, such as secondary education, pre-sessional programs, or workplace English settings. This dominance of higher education also suggests that current evidence reflects the experiences of relatively autonomous learners with intermediate-to-advanced proficiency levels, potentially overlooking the needs of less proficient or younger learners who may require different scaffolding approaches.

The methodological landscape reveals a field still in its formative stages. The predominance of mixed-methods (50%) and qualitative approaches (44.4%), combined with heavy reliance on self-reported data (77.8% interviews, 38.9% questionnaires), indicates that current evidence is largely exploratory and perceptual. Such approaches are appropriate for an emerging research area, but they limit claims about the actual impact of GenAI on learning outcomes. The finding that only one study (5.6%) employed a purely quantitative design, and that 44.4% of studies did not specify intervention duration, suggests that the field prioritizes understanding teacher and student experiences over measuring effectiveness. This pattern mirrors findings from systematic reviews on GenAI in language learning more broadly (Lee et al., 2025), suggesting a need for more outcome-focused research designs in EAP-specific contexts.

The affordances identified in this review cluster around efficiency and personalization. These benefits directly address challenges in EAP materials development. Teachers' reports of time savings (44.4% of studies) and capacity to tailor materials to learner proficiency (33.3%) suggest that GenAI can democratize access to high-quality, discipline-specific materials, particularly for instructors working in resource-constrained contexts or without access to specialized corpus tools. The emergence of corpus-informed GenAI platforms, such as CorpusChat (Cheung & Crosthwaite, 2025), represents a promising synthesis of the rigor of corpus linguistics with the accessibility of GenAI. However, the fact that quality improvements were reported in only 27.8% of studies, and that linguistic concerns (over-simplification, unnatural outputs) appeared in 22.2% of studies, suggests that efficiency gains may sometimes come at the expense of authenticity. This consideration is important for EAP, where exposure to genuine disciplinary discourse is essential.

Challenges cluster into three interrelated domains: accuracy (50% hallucinations), ethics (38.9% academic integrity concerns), and pedagogy (33.3% over-reliance). These findings align with discussions of GenAI in education (Crosthwaite, 2025; Gabay et al., 2025), but take on significance in EAP contexts where materials serve as models of disciplinary discourse. When teachers use GenAI to generate reading texts or writing prompts, hallucinations or formulaic outputs may misrepresent disciplinary conventions, potentially undermining the preparation of learners for authentic academic communication. The prominence of prompt dependency (33.3%) as a technical challenge further suggests that effective GenAI integration requires not only technological access but also pedagogical content knowledge and prompt engineering skills. Many EAP practitioners may not have systematically developed these competencies.

The implications drawn from reviewed studies emphasize teacher practice (66.7%) and professional development (50.0%) over technical solutions, reflecting recognition that the impact of GenAI depends on how teachers integrate it into their pedagogical practices. The call for ethical practice guidelines (50.0%) and institutional support (44.4%) suggests that individual teacher agency is insufficient without structural support. This finding aligns with conceptual work arguing that successful AI integration requires multi-level support spanning individual, institutional, and policy domains (Crosthwaite, 2025; Zhang & Liu, 2026).

## 5.2. Research gaps and future directions

Despite the contributions of the 18 reviewed studies, gaps remain that point to four priority areas for future research.

The exclusive focus on higher education represents a critical contextual gap. EAP instruction occurs across diverse settings, including pre-sessional programs, pathway colleges, secondary schools preparing for English-medium instruction, and workplace contexts. Each setting involves different learner profiles, institutional constraints, and pedagogical goals. Future research should examine how GenAI supports EAP materials development across these varied contexts, particularly in underrepresented settings where teacher workload and resource constraints may be even more acute than in universities. Methodologically, the field would benefit from more longitudinal designs and diverse data sources. Only two studies (11.1%) employed long-term interventions (> 16 weeks), limiting understanding of how GenAI integration evolves over time and whether initial efficiency gains are sustained or whether new challenges emerge with extended use. Future studies should employ longitudinal designs spanning multiple semesters to investigate how teachers' GenAI practices and competencies develop over time, whether students' responses to AI-generated materials change with repeated exposure, and the long-term impact on learning outcomes. Additionally, the heavy reliance on self-reported data suggests a need for more observational and artifact-based approaches, such as classroom video recordings, think-aloud protocols during materials development, and detailed analysis of teacher-AI interaction logs.

The dominance of ChatGPT (50% of studies used it exclusively) reflects market reality but limits understanding of how different GenAI tools might serve distinct EAP materials development functions. Emerging tools offer specialized capabilities: the multimodal integration of Gemini, the extended context windows of Claude, and domain-specific models trained on academic corpora. Future research should compare the effectiveness of different GenAI tools for specific EAP tasks (e.g., reading text simplification vs. writing prompt generation vs. rubric creation) and investigate how tool combinations might optimize materials quality and mitigate the limitations of individual tools.

Reviewed studies documented various GenAI applications, but few provided theoretically grounded frameworks for integrating AI-generated materials into coherent instructional sequences. Future research should develop and test instructional design models that specify when and how teachers should use GenAI at different stages of materials development (planning, creation, adaptation, evaluation), how AI-generated materials should be sequenced with other learning resources, and what scaffolding learners need to effectively engage with AI-generated materials. Such frameworks would move beyond tool-centric descriptions toward principled pedagogical integration. The reviewed studies portrayed teachers using GenAI in various roles (co-creator, assistant, quality checker) but did not systematically examine which collaboration patterns yield optimal outcomes. Future qualitative research employing detailed case studies, design-based research, or conversation analysis of teacher-AI interactions could illuminate how teachers negotiate agency with AI tools, what decision-making processes guide acceptance, modification, or rejection of AI outputs, and how the pedagogical content knowledge of teachers interacts with prompt engineering skills to shape materials quality. Only a minority of reviewed studies examined student responses to GenAI-created materials, and those that did relied primarily on self-reports. Future research should employ diverse methods (e.g., learning analytics, eye-tracking, stimulated recall interviews) to investigate how learners perceive the authenticity and usefulness of AI-generated materials compared to teacher-created or published materials, whether learners can distinguish AI-generated from human-created content, and how learner engagement and outcomes vary across different types of AI-generated materials.

The field lacks systematic frameworks for evaluating the quality of AI-generated EAP materials. Reviewed studies mentioned concerns about accuracy, authenticity, and pedagogical appropriateness, but no validated rubrics or evaluation protocols emerged. Future research should develop and validate quality assurance frameworks that address linguistic quality (grammatical accuracy, register appropriateness, disciplinary discourse features), pedagogical quality (alignment with learning objectives, scaffolding adequacy, cognitive demand), and ethical quality (transparency about AI use, copyright compliance, bias detection). Such frameworks would support both teachers evaluating AI outputs and researchers comparing materials across studies. The emergence of corpus-informed GenAI tools (Cheung & Crosthwaite, 2025; Zhu et al., 2025) suggests a promising direction, but systematic evidence is needed on whether corpus grounding actually improves materials quality. Comparative studies should examine whether corpus-grounded GenAI outputs exhibit more authentic disciplinary discourse features than standalone GenAI, whether learners exposed to corpus-grounded materials demonstrate better preparation for authentic academic tasks, and what corpus features (e.g., discipline-specificity, genre representation, recency) most strongly predict materials quality.

The strong emphasis on professional development needs (50% of studies) raises questions about what effective AI literacy training looks like for EAP practitioners. Future research should design and evaluate PD models that address prompt engineering for EAP-specific tasks, critical evaluation of AI outputs for disciplinary accuracy, ethical integration strategies that model responsible AI use for students, and collaborative approaches where teachers share prompts, outputs, and adaptation strategies. Comparative studies could examine different PD formats (workshops, communities of practice, self-paced modules) and their impact on the GenAI integration practices of teachers. The call for institutional support (44.4%) suggests that individual teachers face structural barriers that PD alone cannot address. Future research should investigate what institutional policies best support responsible GenAI use in EAP programs, how resource allocation (e.g., paid GenAI subscriptions, release time for materials development) affects integration quality, and what forms of administrative leadership facilitate sustainable GenAI integration. Cross-institutional comparative studies could illuminate how different policy environments shape teacher practices and student outcomes. Learning inequality was rarely mentioned in reviewed studies, but differential access to GenAI tools may exacerbate existing educational disparities. Future research should examine how socioeconomic factors affect teachers' and students' access to GenAI tools, whether institutional provision of tools reduces or reproduces inequalities, and how GenAI integration affects learners from diverse linguistic and educational backgrounds.

## 5.3. Theoretical and pedagogical implications

This review contributes to theorization of AI-mediated language teaching by highlighting the characteristics of EAP materials development as a domain of AI application. Unlike language learning contexts, EAP involves discourse conventions, authenticity requirements, and preparation for academic communication. These characteristics suggest that theoretical frameworks developed for general AI-language learning integration (e.g., sociocultural perspectives on human-AI collaboration, activity theory analyses of tool mediation) must be adapted to account for the unique demands of EAP. Future theoretical work should articulate how GenAI mediates the relationship between teachers, learners, and disciplinary discourse communities.

For EAP practitioners, this review suggests recommendations. Teachers new to GenAI should begin with low-stakes applications, such as generating exercise variants or creating rubric drafts, before progressing to high-stakes materials like summative assessment items or core reading texts. Developing prompt engineering expertise is essential, requiring investment in learning prompting strategies that include providing context, specifying conventions, and requesting multiple iterations. Critical oversight must be maintained by always verifying AI outputs for factual accuracy, disciplinary appropriateness, and alignment with learning objectives. AI-generated materials should never be used without human review. Where possible, teachers should combine AI with corpus tools, using corpus-informed GenAI platforms or cross-checking AI outputs against authentic disciplinary corpora to ensure discourse authenticity. Modeling ethical AI use is also important. Teachers should be transparent with students about when and how they use GenAI and explicitly teach critical AI literacy skills alongside content instruction. Finally, teachers can collaborate with colleagues by sharing prompts, outputs, and adaptation strategies within professional communities to build collective expertise and reduce individual workload.

For institutions and policymakers, this review highlights the need for sustainable resource allocation, professional development, clear policy frameworks, and communities of practice. Institutions should provide paid GenAI subscriptions for teachers, recognizing that the limitations of free versions may compromise materials quality. Professional development opportunities should go beyond tool demonstrations to address pedagogical integration, ethical considerations, and quality assurance. Clear institutional guidelines on acceptable AI use in materials development are needed, addressing copyright, disclosure, and quality standards. Supporting teacher communities where EAP practitioners can share experiences, troubleshoot challenges, and develop practices represents an investment in sustainable GenAI integration.

# 6. Conclusion

This systematic review examined 18 empirical studies on GenAI use in EAP materials development, providing a synthesis of research trends, affordances, challenges, and implications. Our findings reveal a field in rapid evolution, characterized by exclusive focus on higher education contexts, predominance of mixed-methods and qualitative approaches, and reliance on self-reported data. ChatGPT emerged as the dominant tool, while efficiency and personalization were identified as affordances. Accuracy concerns, ethical challenges, and pedagogical limitations represented barriers to effective integration. These findings align with systematic reviews on GenAI in language education (Lee et al., 2025; Crosthwaite, 2025), yet also highlight the demands of EAP contexts where materials must model authentic disciplinary discourse.

The review identified four priority directions for future research. First, the field requires more research designs and contexts, including longitudinal studies, varied educational settings beyond higher education, and investigation of multiple GenAI tools beyond ChatGPT. Second, investigation of pedagogical integration is needed, particularly instructional design frameworks, teacher-AI collaboration patterns, and learner perspectives on AI-generated materials. Third, quality assurance and validation research must address the development of materials quality frameworks and corpus-grounded validation approaches. Fourth, institutional and policy-level research should examine professional development models, policy frameworks, and equity and access issues. These directions respond to the current limitations of the field and build on the contributions of existing studies.

Several implications emerge from this review. For researchers, findings suggest the need to move beyond exploratory, perceptual studies toward outcome-focused designs that measure the impact of GenAI on learning. The development of theoretically grounded instructional design frameworks and quality assurance protocols represents a priority. For EAP practitioners, the review suggests starting with low-stakes applications, developing prompt engineering expertise, maintaining critical oversight, combining AI with corpus tools, modeling ethical AI use, and collaborating with colleagues. For institutions and policymakers, sustainable resource allocation, professional development, clear policy frameworks, and communities of practice support responsible GenAI integration (Ngo & Hastie, 2025; Zhang & Liu, 2026).

This review has several limitations. First, we searched two major databases (Web of Science and SCOPUS), but relevant studies may exist in other databases or in publications not indexed in these sources. Second, our search terms may not have captured all relevant studies, particularly those using different terminology for materials development (e.g., "curriculum design," "resource creation"). Third, the inclusion of only peer-reviewed journal articles may have excluded valuable insights from conference proceedings, book chapters, or practitioner publications. Fourth, the rapidly evolving nature of GenAI technology means that findings from studies conducted in 2023 may already be outdated as tools improve. Finally, our focus on empirical studies means that conceptual and theoretical contributions to understanding GenAI in EAP materials development were not examined.

Despite these limitations, this review makes several contributions to the growing body of literature on GenAI in language education. By focusing specifically on EAP materials development, a core practitioner activity that has received limited systematic attention, this review provides evidence to inform pedagogical decisions, institutional policies, and future research agendas. As GenAI technology continues to advance and become more deeply integrated into educational contexts, ongoing systematic reviews will be necessary to capture emerging trends, validate initial findings, and identify new research directions. We hope this review serves as a foundation for such ongoing scholarly conversation.
