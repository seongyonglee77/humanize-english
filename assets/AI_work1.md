# **Enhancing pre-service teacher agency through SQD2 strategies: Distributed agency in designing GenAI-integrated XR classrooms**

## Abstract
Although pre-service teacher (PST) education has addressed generative AI (GenAI) and extended reality (XR) integration, little is known about how the course design shapes the development of teacher agency within the context of integrated emerging technologies. Drawing on ecological and sociomaterial perspectives, this study examined how Synthesis of Qualitative Evidence (SQD) 2 strategies mediated distributed agency development among 53 EFL PSTs who engaged in a 16-week GenAI and XR classroom design at a South Korean university. Using thematic analysis, we analyzed group discussions, focus-group interviews, and reflective papers as the primary qualitative dataset, while teaching demonstrations conducted in the 3D Virtual classroom and participants' design artifacts were used as supporting evidence for triangulation and illustrative figures. We identified seven themes from the data: distributed affordance noticing, human–AI co-authoring, spatial–embodied agency, material resistance for adaptive agency, distributed pedagogical judgment, professional identity shifts, and authentic co-orchestration. SQD2 strategies served as orchestrating conditions that activated their affordance perception, enabled evaluative agency in human–AI co-production, and supported professional identity construction. From the findings, we propose an aggregative entanglement model of distributed agency in GenAI–XR ecologies: while individual human and non-human contributions remain traceable, collective outcomes emerge through relational entanglement and exceed what any single agent could produce alone. We also offer implications for teacher education, emphasizing the significance of PSTs' evaluative agency and orchestration capacities through pedagogical scaffolding, collaborative design, and SQD2 strategies in multi-tool integration contexts.

Keywords: distributed agency, pre-service teacher education, generative AI, extended reality, synthesis of qualitative evidence, sociomateriality 

## 1. Introduction
The integration of generative AI (GenAI) and extended reality (XR) into teacher education marks a fundamental shift in the conditions under which professional agency develops (Author, 2022; Godwin-Jones, 2024). Earlier technology integration contexts demanded tool mastery and technological knowledge for using web-based tools, digital portfolios, and learning management systems, as well as adopting their pedagogical affordances to manage instructional goals (Author, 2024a; Koehler & Mishra, 2005). GenAI–XR ecologies, however, impose a different requirement, as non-human actors generate content, constrain design possibilities, redirect pedagogical judgment, and respond to user input in ways that reshape the learning ecology in real time (Orlikowski, 2007; Schatzki, 2025). Pre-service teachers (PSTs) entering these environments need to develop the capacity to act purposefully within human–AI–XR assemblages (Schatzki, 2025), in which agency is co-constituted across pedagogical intent, GenAI output, spatial XR design, and peer collaboration (Tan et al., 2025).

This demand points to distributed agency, the emergent, relational, and sociomaterial capacity to act arising from dynamic interactions among humans, technologies, artifacts, and environments (Godwin-Jones, 2024). Drawing on a sociocultural perspective (Lantolf & Thorne, 2006; Vygotsky, 1978), an ecological approach to affordances (Gibson, 1979; van Lier, 2008), actor-network and sociomaterial perspectives (Latour, 2005; Orlikowski, 2007), distributed cognition (Hutchins, 1995), and relational and collective dimensions (Archer, 2000; Edwards, 2011; Priestley et al., 2015), distributed agency posits that agency is enacted, rather than possessed, through dynamic interactions within specific arrangements of people, tools, and material conditions (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Högström et al., 2024; Schatzki, 2025). In GenAI–XR teacher education, this ecology demands that PSTs simultaneously manage AI-generated content, negotiate spatial XR design choices, collaborate with peers across different roles, and reconstruct professional identity in relation to the affordances of specific tools that do not behave predictably (Author, 2025c; Darvin, 2025; Tan et al., 2025). To develop professional agency within such an ecology, teacher education needs to provide more than access to tools and also establish the relational conditions under which distributed agency can emerge in a given pedagogical condition (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Tondeur et al., 2025).

However, teacher education programs have not yet fully theorized what forms of agency GenAI–XR ecologies require and how preparation can develop them (Author, 2025b, 2026; Darvin, 2025). Established frameworks, such as Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK, Koehler & Mishra, 2005) and Technology Acceptance Model (Author, 2025a), were designed for relatively stable, individual technology use contexts, focusing on individual competence, attitudes, and behavioral intentions, not for the emergent, relational, sociomaterial character of agency enacted within complex technological assemblages where non-human actors exert influence on teaching (Darvin, 2025; Priestley et al., 2015). That is, TPACK scores do not show whether a PST can evaluate and align AI-generated content with specific pedagogical principles, adapt a VR room layout in response to an unexpected technical constraint, or sustain pedagogical coherence while orchestrating peer avatars and AI-generated scripts simultaneously (Author, 2025c; Darvin, 2025; Priestley et al., 2015). In fact, these competencies require a construct that focuses on relationality, emergence, and sociomateriality in local teaching contexts (Orlikowski, 2007; Schatzki, 2025).

Three gaps in the existing literature have motivated this study. First, while research on GenAI and XR in teacher education has developed largely in parallel, few studies have examined their combined ecology in which AI-generated content populates immersive virtual spaces co-designed by PSTs (Author, 2025a; Kong et al., 2025). This gap is significant, as combined GenAI–XR conditions may offer promising outcomes relative to single-technology conditions, including potentially higher engagement, more authentic collaborative lesson designs, and stronger affective outcomes, yet the theoretical description of why this may occur and what forms of professional agency it may require remains underexplored (Author, 2025b; Yang et al., 2025). Second, the studies that address teacher agency in technology-mediated contexts typically operationalize agency as an individual attribute rather than as an emergent achievement co-produced through human–tool–peer assemblages (Darvin, 2025; Godwin-Jones, 2024), potentially overlooking the distributed, relational, and sociomaterial constitution of agency that matters most in complex technological ecologies (Orlikowski, 2007). As Schatzki (2025) argued, even when individual contributions are traceable within a distributed system, the products by human–AI–VR assemblages are irreducibly emergent, co-constituted through their relational interdependence rather than through summing individual inputs. Third, although the Synthesis of Qualitative Evidence (SQD) 2 framework (Tondeur et al., 2025) updates established strategies for preparing PSTs to teach with digital technologies and may therefore be one of the most current approaches for designing GenAI–XR teacher preparation, its role in mediating distributed agency within integrated GenAI–XR ecologies remains empirically underdeveloped (Author, 2022; cf. Buchner & Hofmann, 2022). In particular, the mechanisms through which SQD2 strategies enable distributed agency to emerge have not yet been explored.

Therefore, adopting ecological perspectives on teacher agency (Priestley et al., 2015) and distributed agency that integrates sociomaterial views (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Orlikowski, 2007), this study aims to address these gaps by exploring how distributed agency is mediated, enacted, and negotiated in GenAI–XR–integrated teacher-education contexts and by specifying the role of SQD2 in that process. This research focus is important, as examining distributed agency in a GenAI–XR ecology is expected to strengthen theoretical grounds and pedagogical design in technology-mediated teacher preparation. Specifically, the study investigates a 16-week GenAI-integrated XR classroom design that involves EFL PSTs at a South Korean university, examining how SQD2 strategies shape the mediation, enactment, and negotiation of distributed agency.

## 2. Literature review

### 2.1. Teacher agency, affordance, and distributed agency

The conceptualization of teacher agency has shifted from an individual psychological property to an emergent ecological achievement (Priestley et al., 2015; van Lier, 2008). Within this ecological view, Priestley et al. (2015) defined agency as achieved "through the interplay of personal capacities and the resources, affordances and constraints of the environment" (p. 19) and identified three temporal dimensions: iterational (past experiences), projective (future orientations), and practical-evaluative (present pedagogical judgment). Extending this ecological stance to social positioning, Archer (2000) distinguished primary agents (passively positioned) from corporate agents (reflexively and collectively acting), implying that teacher education should support PSTs in moving from passive actors to reflexive professional ones. Author (2024a) added that shifts in agency enactment are shaped by contextual factors, such as assessment culture, parental expectations, and institutional support for professional development (PD), rather than individual factors alone.

Affordance theory further provides a useful analytic framework that links agency to the technological environment (Gibson, 1979). Building on Gibson's (1979) ecological approach, van Lier (2008) conceptualized affordances as relational possibilities for action that connect perception with action and are realized through interaction within and beyond the classroom. Liu and Chao (2018) refined this approach by demonstrating that teacher presence, affordance perceivability, and agency are interdependent processes in which teachers who perceive more affordances can create conditions for learner agency. Importantly, affordance perceivability is not a fixed individual trait but a learnable, socially mediated capacity (Author, 2022; Liu & Chao, 2018). For example, Author (2022) reported four pedagogical affordance categories, including communication, engagement, constructability, and multimodal sharing, that emerged through PSTs' collaborative noticing episodes in a virtual environment development project, categories that individual exploration may not fully capture.

These ecological and affordance perspectives converge in distributed agency, comprehensively theorized through a sociomaterial view (Orlikowski, 2007; Schatzki, 2025). From this view, educational practices are constitutively entangled: neither human actors nor material artifacts pre-exist one another but are mutually constituted through situated practice (Orlikowski, 2007). Agency, accordingly, can be understood as a relational, emergent, and shifting achievement realized through associations of human and non-human elements (Orlikowski, 2007). In GenAI–XR ecologies, non-human actors, including AI-generated outputs, spatial VR constraints, and algorithmic affordances, may redirect and reformulate pedagogical judgment in the moment of practice as active participants rather than passive instruments awaiting human direction (Schatzki, 2025). Schatzki (2025) further distinguishes acting (intentional and purposeful, paradigmatically human), doing (attributable to any entity via its effects), and effecting (making a difference to the world as causality) to clarify how entities contribute to practice, arguing that in most nexuses involving human actors, individual contributions can remain epistemically traceable even as collective outcomes may exceed what any single contributor yields. In educational contexts, Sarkio et al. (2023) traced the entanglement of school spaces, digital instruments, and pedagogic activities, suggesting that affordances and constraints may be inseparable from teacher agency across place, tools, and practice. Godwin-Jones (2024) extended this sociomaterial perspective to GenAI as a "shifting entanglement of humans and nonhumans" (Hasse, 2019, p. 360, as cited in Godwin-Jones, 2024), while Darvin (2025) expanded this to include coded inequity, whereby training data biases encode ideologies of race, gender, and Western-centrism, as a dimension of sociomaterial constraint that responsible AI users must navigate. Despite these theoretical developments, empirical studies describing how PSTs develop distributed agency within integrated emerging technology ecologies remain limited. Against this conceptual backdrop, teacher education strategies, such as SQD2, should be reviewed as orchestrating conditions for distributed agency in GenAI–XR ecologies.

### 2.2. SQD2-informed design-based teacher education

SQD2 (Tondeur et al., 2025) provides a current framework for designing teacher preparation in the AI context. The original SQD (SQD1) model (Tondeur et al., 2012), drawn from 19 qualitative studies, identified seven micro-level strategies, including modeling technology use, reflection, instructional design models, collaborative learning, authentic experiences, feedback, and content-specific technology use. Findings from this synthesis indicated that simple technology integration is insufficient without design-based training when most institutions in the world offered ICT courses but PSTs still did not feel well-prepared for technology use. Thus, SQD2 (Tondeur et al., 2025) extends these strategies to encompass design thinking, AI-mediated pedagogy, and three additional dimensions, such as digital identity development, the affective dimension of technology integration, and AI as a reflective scaffold for professional learning. Specifically, digital identity development is conceptualized as a continuous process of interpreting professional values and practices regarding evolving digital contexts, moving PSTs from viewing technology as supplementary to integrating it as constitutive of their professional identity. The affective dimension addresses not only attitudes and beliefs but also the emotional sphere of technology engagement, recognizing that anxiety, frustration, and sensitivity to technology gaps among learners can constrain integration as consequentially as limited technical skill. Lastly, AI as a reflective scaffold refers to the use of AI-generated analysis to support metacognitive monitoring and iterative revision of pedagogical thinking, with the aim of linking human reflection with technology-mediated professional learning.

SQD2 strategies may work as a mechanism for distributed agency development, with evidence that speaks to both strategy intensity and the mechanisms through which strategies operate (Tondeur et al., 2025; Buchner & Hofmann, 2022). Regarding the intensity, Buchner and Hofmann (2022) showed that the group who adopted a full set of strategies outperformed the reduced-strategy group on will, skill, and tool dimensions in a comparison of two SQD-based AR/VR training designs. As mechanism-oriented evidence, Cabbeke et al. (2025) examined an SQD2-based collaborative design course focused on authentic design challenges and highlighted how collaborative engagement can situate learning socially, strengthen teachers’ sense of agency in reshaping their practice, and support iterative learning through testing and redesign. They further showed that SQD2 strategies "function dynamically rather than in isolation" (p. 1348) and serve as interdependent orchestrating conditions, with collaboration, feedback, and modeling scaffolding the instructional design process. In a related context, Zhou et al. (2025) addressed GenAI-mediated language teacher PD and reported that continued engagement with SQD strategies supports a developmental trajectory from initial tool use toward pedagogical redesign, alongside a shift in how teachers understand GenAI, from a productivity tool to a resource for rethinking their teaching practices.

Some empirical studies have examined SQD in XR-mediated teacher education. For example, Author (2022) applied SQD1 in a desktop VR context, identifying affordance noticing and collaborative design as key sites of agency development. Author (2024b) expanded this to AI chatbot-integrated lesson planning, finding that high-TPACK PSTs integrated chatbots systematically while low-TPACK PSTs used them as add-ons. Despite these studies, two gaps remain. First, the updated SQD2 strategies have not been examined in integrated GenAI–XR ecologies, and the mechanisms through which SQD2 enables distributed agency in such ecologies remain underexplored (Author, 2025b; Tondeur et al., 2025). Second, studies that document dynamic SQD processes in collaborative design (Cabbeke et al., 2025) and GenAI-mediated teacher PD (Zhou et al., 2025) do not address integrated GenAI–XR ecologies or conceptualise agency as a distributed sociomaterial achievement. This study addresses these gaps.

### 2.3. GenAI and XR as integrated design ecologies

GenAI and XR offer different pedagogical affordances, but they may form a design ecology that extends what either can support on its own (Author, 2025a, 2025b; Bower, 2019). In teacher education, GenAI may be especially relevant for rapid content generation, iterative co-construction through prompting, and the development of prompt literacy as a professional competence (Bower et al., 2024; Darvin, 2025; Moorhouse et al., 2024). In particular, Hwang, Lee, and Shin (2025) describe prompt iteration as a metacognitive process of monitoring mismatches between intended and generated outputs and strategically revising prompts, suggesting that PSTs can use GenAI to reveal tacit pedagogical thinking. Darvin's (2025) taxonomy further presents GenAI as supporting a wide range of ELT functions, while it introduces constraints such as hallucination, context blindness, and coded inequity that cannot be addressed through technical fixes alone and therefore require evaluative agency. At the same time, these affordances may not emerge automatically through access alone. Longitudinal studies by Zhou et al. (2025) suggest that more advanced uses of GenAI tend to develop gradually through guided mentoring and authentic implementation, indicating that more demanding pedagogical possibilities of GenAI may become available under ecologically supportive conditions.

Research shows that 3D virtual environments, including VR, contribute diverse affordances unavailable in 2D interfaces (Makransky & Petersen, 2021; Wu & Chun, 2025). Makransky and Petersen's (2021) Cognitive-Affective Model of Immersive Learning identifies presence and agency as the primary mechanisms through which VR enhances learning outcomes, observing that high presence can increase extraneous cognitive load when spatial complexity is unscaffolded, thereby potentially posing a design risk directly addressed in the SQD2 exploration stage. Wu and Chun (2025) demonstrated that Taiwanese PSTs could design immersive VR lessons, presenting two sets of affordances within an iVR-supported training context: three types of presence (physical presence, social presence, and self-presence) and two forms of agency (action control and environment modification). Lee and Wu (2024) added that embodied rehearsal in physically navigating designed VR spaces from the student perspective can afford spatial reasoning and design revision that text-based planning cannot support.

Prior work suggests that combining AI with VR can create pedagogical conditions that a single tool may not be able to support as effectively on its own (Author, 2025b; Kim et al., 2022). In their comparative study, Author (2025a) reported that the integrated condition was associated with more favorable perceptions across a range of technology acceptance dimensions, especially those on engagement and immersion. A related line of research in EFL teacher education by Author (2025b) also indicates that combining AI chatbot and metaverse environments may support more positive affective outcomes than single-tool designs. Yet these studies largely report perceived benefits of the combined setting, leaving underexamined how PSTs actually exercise agency within GenAI–XR ecologies through a cycle of design, negotiation, and enactment.

### 2.4. From collaboration to orchestration: PSTs' professional judgment, identity development, and distributed agency in action

For PSTs, integrating GenAI and XR is not only a matter of tool use but also a matter of how they reason with others, develop professional identities, and coordinate action during teaching. These capacities emerge through interaction with peers, tools, prompts, spatial arrangements, and the shifting demands of enactment, which is central to understanding how distributed agency is formed and exercised (Author, 2025c; Butler et al., 2015; Edwards, 2011). In GenAI–XR teaching ecologies, these processes become especially important because pedagogical decisions are usually made through collaboration (Cabbeke et al., 2025).

Training PSTs in professional judgment may begin in collaborative design work, where they recognize what peers and tools can offer and respond to issues with shared pedagogical goals (Edwards, 2011). In this sense, collaboration is not merely social support but a site of distributed pedagogical reasoning, as it allows ideas to be co-constructed, challenged, revised, and redistributed across group members and mediating tools. However, this relational capacity does not develop automatically: Butler et al. (2015) showed that collaborative inquiry still depends on enabling conditions beyond peer interaction alone, while Author (2025c) identified agentive collaboration and responsible management as key engagement patterns in GenAI-specific design. Judgment in GenAI–XR ecologies is therefore better understood as coordinated evaluation formed by peer scaffolding, subject knowledge, and ongoing negotiation with tool limitations rather than as individual decision-making alone (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Högström et al., 2024).

Identity development is also significant to consider regarding teacher collaboration, as agency in GenAI–XR ecologies develops through changing role perceptions as well as through action. As PSTs work with prompts, generated outputs, peer feedback, and simulated teaching environments, they do not simply learn how to use new tools; instead, they also negotiate what it means to act as a teacher, designer, evaluator, and facilitator (Darvin, 2025; Lin et al., 2025). Darvin (2025) and Tondeur et al. (2025) suggested that confidence, anxiety, and role perceptions shape how teachers notice AI affordances and persist through constraints. Lin et al. (2025) likewise identified a movement from function-centered to pedagogy-centered and learner-centered engagement, indicating that ongoing work with GenAI can reconstruct how PSTs understand themselves as teachers and designers rather than as tool users alone (Chen, 2023; Lin et al., 2025).

Orchestration brings these processes into teaching itself: it refers to the real-time coordination of tools, learners, tasks, and environments under conditions that require continuous adjustment rather than simply executing a plan (Dillenbourg & Jermann, 2010; Roschelle et al., 2013). Whereas collaborative work involves horizontal, peer-mediated co-construction of ideas, plans, and materials during design work, orchestration demands an additional layer of executive agency; that is, it requires a teacher to coordinate collaboratively produced resources, including spatial arrangements, AI-generated materials, and peer roles, which requires in-the-moment judgment under time pressure (Roschelle et al., 2013). This challenge is particularly significant in GenAI–XR contexts, where PSTs should respond to pedagogical demands from students as well as AI-generated suggestions, prompt revisions, virtual spatial arrangements, and peer participation (Author, 2025a, 2025b). Kong et al. (2025) suggested that even under favorable conditions, human-AI collaboration in classrooms may remain limited, indicating that coordination itself may be a major pedagogical challenge, not a guaranteed outcome of technology integration. Author (2025b) similarly suggested that micro-teaching in integrated environments may distribute action across AI-generated scripts, spatial layouts, and peer avatars. Orchestration is therefore the point at which distributed agency moves from collaborative preparation into situated teaching practice (Roschelle et al., 2013).

Previous studies on emerging technologies for teaching indicate that combining AI and XR may offer pedagogical benefits, but they offer limited explanation of how these affordances are related to distributed agency in practice. This observation points to three gaps. First, research on GenAI and XR in teacher education has developed largely in parallel, with GenAI studies often focusing on prompt work and teacher perceptions and XR studies focusing on presence, immersion, and spatial design, leaving underexamined their combined ecology in which AI-generated content is integrated into virtual spaces co-designed by PSTs (Author, 2025a; Makransky & Petersen, 2021; Yang et al., 2025). Second, within this line of research, teacher agency in technology-mediated contexts has been largely explored as an individual practice rather than as a sociomaterial achievement co-produced through human–tool–peer assemblages, potentially overlooking the collective component of agency that matters most in technology-enhanced teacher training (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Orlikowski, 2007). Third, although the SQD2 framework (Tondeur et al., 2025) may represent one of the most recent approaches for designing AI-integrated teacher preparation, the mechanisms through which SQD2 strategies enable distributed agency to emerge remain empirically underexplored. Therefore, this study aims to examine how distributed agency is mediated, enacted, and negotiated in a GenAI–XR design ecology and how SQD2 supports that process, according to the following research questions (RQs).

RQ1: How do SQD2 strategies mediate the emergence of distributed agency within the GenAI–XR design cycle?

RQ2: What forms and features of distributed agency emerge across design, feedback, and teaching demonstration phases?

RQ3: How do PSTs notice, negotiate, and adapt to GenAI and XR affordances and constraints in relation to pedagogical decision-making and professional identity development?

## 3. Methods

### 3.1. Research design

Building on previous studies about the ecological and sociomaterial perspectives on teacher agency (Orlikowski, 2007; Priestley et al., 2015; Schatzki, 2025), the study adopted an exploratory qualitative design with a constructivist orientation, as distributed agency in GenAI–XR teacher education ecologies remains underexplored in language education. Drawing on a design-based research orientation (Tondeur et al., 2012, 2025), the study focused on how distributed agency develops through iterative engagement with GenAI–XR design tasks: this orientation was appropriate because the intervention involved repeated cycles of design, enactment, feedback, and revision across the semester.

### 3.2. Context and participants

The study was conducted in a 16-week course, titled "Language Teaching Methods" at a South Korean university, designed to integrate communicative language teaching (CLT) principles with technology use for EFL instruction. This course was the first implementation of SQD2-mediated GenAI–XR design activities in the English Education program at this institution, making it an appropriate site for investigating how distributed agency emerges in this context. Fifty-three EFL PSTs participated (34 female, 19 male; age mean = 21.4 years, SD = 0.8), organized into 23 collaborative design groups of three to four members each through purposive sampling based on course enrollment. They reported little prior use of GenAI tools or VR design platforms for teaching purposes, allowing the analysis to focus on learning processes within the SQD2 cycle. Following institutional ethics approval obtained prior to data collection, all participants provided informed consent to take part in the study. Participant data were anonymised using unique numerical identifiers, while identifying information was stored separately in a secure database.

### 3.3. Research procedure: SQD2-mediated design cycle

The 16-week intervention applied all seven SQD2 strategies, as suggested in Tondeur et al. (2025), including modeling technology use, exploration, collaboration, design-based learning, authentic experiences, feedback, and reflective practice, across three phases: Preparation (Weeks 1–2), VR and Lesson Design with GenAI (Weeks 3–14), and Teaching Demonstration (Weeks 15–16), as illustrated in Fig. 1. Corresponding lectures and feedback sessions were offered to deliver CLT pedagogy principles as subject-matter knowledge throughout the intervention, providing the pedagogical framework of language education within which participants' design decisions were evaluated.

**Fig. 1.** Research procedure.

ChatGPT-4o served as the GenAI tool, allowing PSTs to co-work on lesson plans, dialogue exchanges, and teaching scripts through iterative prompting cycles (Hwang, Lee, & Shin, 2025). SPOT Virtual as a desktop VR platform was additionally used as the classroom platform, enabling PST groups to build virtual learning environments from scratch, embed AI-generated teaching materials directly into the space, and configure interactive objects for learners to engage with in embodied tasks (Author, 2025a). As illustrated in Fig. 2, the spatial and interactive properties of the SPOT environment allowed PSTs to implement lesson plans as embodied instructional sequences within which learners could navigate, interact, and engage with pedagogically designed content.

**Fig. 2.** Design and operation system of Spot Virtual.

In the Preparation Phase (Weeks 1–2), the instructor modeled effective prompting strategies for ChatGPT and design techniques with SPOT Virtual, using annotated exemplar VR classrooms to illustrate how AI-generated content, as well as interactive objects, integrates into spatial learning environments (see Fig. 1). At the same time, PST groups explored the affordances of SPOT Virtual, tested initial AI prompts, and produced design plans as drafts.

The VR and Lesson Design with GenAI Phase (Weeks 3–14) included three iterative steps: Draft, modification, and final product. During the Draft stage (Weeks 3–6), the PST groups produced initial VR layouts and design objectives while co-authoring lesson plan drafts with ChatGPT. The tutor's instruction covered CLT interactive techniques and design logic. During the Modification stage (Weeks 7–10), the students refined VR environments by adding interactive objects and task sequences, revised lesson plans with pedagogical reasoning, and completed a mid-term examination assessing their CLT understanding during design work. During the Final Product stage (Weeks 11–14), they developed game-based activities, fully integrated AI-generated materials (e.g., images, dialogue exchanges, task instructions) into their VR environments, and finalized teaching scripts co-authored with ChatGPT. All three steps followed the same iterative structure of design, critique, and revision: group design work and discussion, a mini-presentation, and a peer and instructor feedback session.

In the Teaching Demonstration Phase (Weeks 15–16), each group conducted a micro-teaching session in their own VR environment, orchestrating AI-generated teaching materials, spatial layouts, peer avatars working as student participants, and interactive objects. As illustrated in Fig. 3, peer groups participated utilizing student avatars, creating authentic, embodied teaching conditions. Feedback from peers and the instructor followed each demonstration, and participants completed reflective papers addressing design decisions, professional identity development, and affective responses to technology engagement.

**Fig. 3.** Groups using student avatars in virtual spaces.

### 3.4. Data collection

Multiple datasets were collected across all three phases to allow for triangulation and documentation of how distributed agency was enacted throughout participants' design cycle. The primary qualitative data sources were group discussions, focus-group interviews, and reflective papers. Group discussions (approximately 65 hours of audio-recorded collaborative design work) documented decision-making processes, collaborative problem-solving, and collective reasoning as groups navigated design challenges. All group discussions were conducted in Korean, transcribed verbatim, and translated into English for analysis. Focus-group interviews were conducted after the project with all 23 groups via Zoom (31–43 minutes per group), exploring components of distributed agency identified in the conceptual framework, including affordance noticing, collaborative reasoning, constraint negotiation, identity shifts, and performative orchestration (see Appendix A for sample interview questions). Reflective papers were completed following teaching demonstrations, using three open-ended prompts (Appendix B) that addressed learning processes, professional identity shifts, affective responses, and pedagogical thinking. Supporting sources were teaching demonstration recordings and design artifacts. Teaching demonstrations were video-recorded to document participants’ in-the-moment decision-making and adaptive pedagogical responses, and selected screenshots were extracted from the recordings. Design artifacts, such as lesson plans, AI prompt logs, and final teaching scripts, were also collected to identify co-authoring decisions, iterative design, and revisions. 

### 3.5 Data analysis

Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2022) was adopted and conducted through six recursive stages of data analysis. We used group discussions, interview transcripts, and reflective papers as the primary sources for analysis, while using design artifacts and teaching demonstration screenshots as supporting evidence to triangulate interpretations. The analysis procedure followed six recursive stages: (1) familiarization through repeated reading of three primary data sources, supplemented by review of three additional sources, (2) initial open coding of the primary dataset for design decisions, tool negotiations, collaborative reasoning processes, identity expressions, affective experiences, and encounters with material constraints (Orlikowski, 2007; Sarkio et al., 2023), (3) theme development by clustering semantically related codes into notable themes, guided by, though not limited to, the six components of distributed agency identified in the conceptual framework (Godwin-Jones, 2024), (4) theme reviews against coded extracts and the primary datasets, while checking against supporting materials whenever relevant, (5) theme definition articulating the scope of each theme, and (6) report production selecting representative extracts and constructing analytic narratives that connected evidence to theoretical interpretation. To identify distributed agency empirically, we developed a coding scheme grounded in the theoretical framework of distributed agency (see Appendix C): it integrated six core dimensions of distributed agency, including affordance noticing, collaborative reasoning, constraint negotiation, identity shifts, affective responses, and performative orchestration (Butler et al., 2015; Godwin-Jones, 2024; Högström et al., 2024; Schatzki, 2025).
For data credibility, inter-rater reliability was established through independent coding by two researchers, with the three datasets treated as a single combined corpus, yielding a Cohen’s kappa of 0.77, indicating substantial agreement.

## 4. Findings

A total of 287 coding units were identified from the qualitative data across seven themes. Distributed noticing of AI–XR affordances constituted the most coded theme (18.1%), followed by human–AI co-authoring (17.4%), authentic co-orchestration (15.7%), socially distributed pedagogical judgment (14.3%), material resistance as an adaptation driver (12.9%), professional identity shifts (11.8%), and spatial–embodied agency (9.8%). The themes are presented in developmental sequence, from initial affordance noticing through collaborative design, constraint negotiation, and identity shifts to performative orchestration, although they operated simultaneously and recursively throughout the intervention. Appendix D provides an overview of themes and their links to distributed agency features, SQD2 strategies, and RQs.

### Theme 1: Distributed noticing of AI–XR affordances

Noticing the pedagogical possibilities of using ChatGPT and SPOT Virtual was a relational, distributed process that emerged primarily through peer interaction (RQ2). Many participants (n = 38) reported that their awareness of patterns in AI-generated outputs, including tendencies toward grammatical explicitness, formal registers, and target-language standardization, developed primarily through collaborative prompting activities. Participants also described how instructor modeling in Phase 1 provided initial perceptual templates and how peer discussion in Phase 2 developed those templates into shared interpretive frameworks before the group articulated the patterns explicitly. For example, one participant described how collective prompting revealed a behavioral tendency of ChatGPT that had not been noticeable in individual use (Extract 1):

> **Extract 1:** At first I just asked ChatGPT for ideas by myself, but when we tested different prompts together in the group, I started noticing some patterns, like how it tends to push certain grammar forms in every response. I would not have seen that alone. It was the group dialogue that helped me understand what the AI does. (#S7, Interview)

Spatial noticing of VR affordances followed a similarly distributed pattern, but the environment itself also shaped what became salient. That is, affordance perception in GenAI–XR ecologies emerged through dynamic interaction among PSTs, an AI tool, and spatial environments. Specifically, some participants (n = 21) described that affordances of SPOT Virtual became apparent through embodied, collective exploration of the virtual classroom, as avatar movement within the designed spaces revealed constraints and possibilities that initial floor plans had not anticipated. As Extract 2 illustrates, this distributed noticing was triggered by a relational encounter between the group, their avatars, and the spatial properties of the environment (also see Fig. 4). They noted that the environment itself participated in the noticing process when PSTs navigated it together, and that technological properties were enacted through practice (Author, 2022; Orlikowski, 2007):

> **Extract 2:** Walking through the VR space with my group made us realize affordances we had not first planned. When the avatar turned the corner, we suddenly understood the room was too small for the listening task. The noticing did not come from just me. It came from the space reacting to all of us together. (#S23, Reflection)

**Fig. 4.** Groups in a listening task room.

### Theme 2: Human–AI co-authoring and distributed decision-making

Data showed that PSTs exercised distributed agency through co-authorship: an evaluative process in which ChatGPT contributed generative capacity while PSTs maintained pedagogical alignment (RQ1, RQ3). Many participants (n = 41) characterized ChatGPT as a collaborator that produced initial lesson scripts, task sequences, and dialogue exchanges faster than individual drafting allowed, but without pedagogical direction. This division of labor between AI for generative speed and PSTs for evaluative judgment was consistently described as productive in the data. Extract 3 illustrates how one group relied on ChatGPT when it created something to evaluate and revise, shifting from viewing GenAI as an autonomous content generator to treating it as a dialogue partner:

> **Extract 3:** We asked ChatGPT to create the first script, but our job was to fix the pedagogical flow. It gave ideas fast, but it did not know our learning goals. So, I think the final design was something we made it with ChatGPT, not something AI just handed to us. (#S31, Interview)

A parallel dimension of distributed agency further emerged within the co-authoring sequences: the exercise of evaluative screening and pedagogical alignment. Some (n = 24) described the process of rejecting, modifying, and re-prompting ChatGPT outputs as the moment when their professional agency became most visible. As Extract 4 shows, the experience of deciding when to accept, redirect, or override AI outputs revealed to the participants that co-authoring was an active professional practice. CLT-based instructional design frameworks provided criteria for evaluating AI outputs, enabling participants to exercise these judgments in a consistent way. This suggested that subject-specific pedagogical knowledge was significant for agentic collaboration with ChatGPT in this context (Moorhouse et al., 2024), preventing uncritical adoption while maintaining the generative contribution of AI.

> **Extract 4:** I realized we were not just using AI. We were making decisions with it. Sometimes the AI idea changed our direction, and sometimes we rejected it. It felt like we shared the design work, but the judgment was on my group. (#S15, Interview)

Such evaluative agency seems to be the professional competency that co-authoring with AI most consistently develops among the participants, the capacity to critically assess and align AI outputs with pedagogical intentions. The design cycle scaffolded this development by positioning co-authoring as a phase-specific task with iterative feedback.

### Theme 3: Spatial–embodied agency in XR design

Agency in XR design emerged through embodied interaction with virtual space, as PSTs treated SPOT Virtual as an active pedagogical participant. The material properties of the environment, including spatial layout, audio zones, object placement, and avatar movement, influenced instructional possibilities (RQ1, RQ2). PST participants (n = 29) reported that designing movement paths, room divisions, and task sequences required a form of spatial reasoning about pedagogy that text-based lesson planning had not elicited from them before. This reasoning was triggered by the experience of navigating the designed space as an avatar and observing how spatial decisions influenced the learning environment. As Extract 5 illustrates, the distinction between planning a lesson and designing it through spatial occupation was salient in the participants' pedagogical decision-making (also see Fig. 5):

> **Extract 5:** When we walked through the VR classroom as avatars, we realized the room division shaped how students would move between tasks. Things we had written in the lesson plan looked fine on paper but did not work in the space. The space was making us redesign the lesson. (#S42, Interview)

**Fig. 5.** 3D classroom design through spatial and avatar-based interaction.

A related but different spatial agency emerged as some PSTs (n = 17) described the VR environment itself as a generative contributor to pedagogical reasoning. Extract 6 illustrates how the material properties of objects in SPOT Virtual, and the ways in which they responded to avatar interaction, generated design insights that PSTs attributed to the space as an active participant (also see Fig. 6). Its technological features were enacted through practice (Orlikowski, 2007), and learner agency in VR could be activated through environmental response (Makransky & Petersen, 2021).

> **Extract 6:** Placing objects in the VR space made me think about the lesson differently. It was not just about content but was like choreography. My sense of what the lesson could come from moving inside the space and observing how the interactive objects reacted to the avatars, for example in the design of a game-based space. (#S18, Reflection)

**Fig. 6.** Quiz space illustrating spatially organised interaction in a virtual classroom.

### Theme 4: Material resistance as an adaptation driver

Although technological constraints are often assumed to hinder learning, material resistance from ChatGPT and SPOT Virtual shaped PSTs' adaptive agency (RQ1, RQ3). Some PSTs (n = 26) reported that AI-related constraints, including hallucinations, culturally inappropriate content, and context blindness, became the most productive design learning moments of the cycle. Each instance required the participants to diagnose what went wrong, refine their prompts, and describe their pedagogical intentions with precision that AI use without resistance would not have demanded. Extract 7 below illustrates how the iterative process of negotiating AI limitations produced a deeper understanding of the tool and clearer pedagogical reasoning, consistent with the productive failure framework (Kapur, 2016).

> **Extract 7:** ChatGPT first misunderstood my prompt and gave me a response that made no sense for our lesson context. So I rewrote the prompt three times. The struggle actually helped me understand the tool better. Also, by the third prompt I was much clearer about what I actually wanted in teaching. Through this, I learned how to negotiate the limits of the AI. (#S9, Reflection)

Data showed that a different type of adaptive agency emerged from constraints related to XR performance. Some students (n = 14) described limitations such as lag during multi-avatar sessions, audio zone failures, and object interaction errors as conditions that led to spatial redesign and improved the final lesson quality. As Extract 8 below shows, such constraints required them to distinguish pedagogically important from peripheral design features, which helped them develop spatial thinking and pedagogical clarity. The combination of AI and XR constraints in this study may indicate compound resistance, a constraint dynamic that demanded simultaneous diagnostic reasoning across two technological systems.

> **Extract 8:** When the VR kept lagging with all of us in the same zone, we had to simplify the task sequence and remove the background. I think the limitation made us rethink what really mattered in the lesson. That was when our team really made decisions together, not just individually. (#S36, Interview)

### Theme 5: Socially distributed pedagogical judgment in collaborative design

Qualitative data revealed that group design activities allowed for collective pedagogical judgment through organic role differentiation, and the pedagogical judgment of the group went beyond what individual contributions could produce (RQ1, RQ2). Many participants (n = 37) reported that their groups developed informal but consistent role assignments across the design cycle. For example, some members specialized in prompt design with ChatGPT, others in spatial layout and object placement in SPOT Virtual, others in pedagogical validation against CLT principles, and others in material production and documentation. These complementary roles distributed cognitive labor across expertise areas while creating collective checking processes that prevented individual oversights from becoming design errors. Extract 9 illustrates how role differentiation in AI and XR design work produced a division of professional labor (Edwards, 2011), which generated outcomes no individual could have produced alone (also see Fig. 7).

> **Extract 9:** I became someone like the prompt engineer while my friend built the VR room and the other checked whether our lesson followed CLT principles. Our work was literally divided across different roles, but it all came together in the final product. The AI and VR work needed different skills and we split them up naturally. (#S5, Interview)

**Fig. 7.** Division of labor in VR space design.

A second pattern concerned the collective reasoning processes that group members developed to evaluate design decisions. Some (n = 22) described collaborative feedback within their groups as the main mechanism through which they developed distributed professional vision, the capacity to attend together to pedagogical alignment, spatial logic, and AI reliability. As Extract 10 illustrates, this collective reasoning developed a shared evaluative perspective through dialogue and exceeded individual analytical capacity. Drawing on relational agency (Edwards, 2011), we observed that recognizing and aligning with what others could offer expanded the shared object of professional activity.

> **Extract 10:** Our ideas about the lesson only made sense after we talked them through as a group. One person saw the AI output as fine, another noticed it did not match our CLT goals, and a third suggested how the VR layout could fix the problem. I could not have predicted that alone. All of us were involved in this thinking process together. (#S27, Group Discussion)

### Theme 6: Professional identity shifts

We further found that affective states and professional identity were integral to core components of distributed agency throughout the design activities, influencing what PSTs noticed, how they responded to constraints, and whether they continued working through challenges (RQ2, RQ3). Some participants (n = 19) described a trajectory from initial technology anxiety or uncertainty toward seeing themselves as teacher-designers. Their positive experiences, such as successful prompting sequences, noticing AI-generated content aligned with CLT goals, and receiving positive recognition from peers, built confidence that enabled further risk-taking. Extract 11 illustrates this trajectory, showing how an initial anxiety response gave way, through specific encounters in the design process, to a strengthened professional identity. Consistent with Aldemir et al. (2025), participants described moving from viewing AI as a productivity tool to treating it as a professional partner in their teaching practices.

> **Extract 11:** I felt really nervous about using ChatGPT and Spot at the beginning. But after going through the process of designing tasks with ChatGPT and fixing what did not work, I started seeing myself differently, as someone who can negotiate with tools rather than avoid them. The course changed how I see my role as a teacher to a different role: classroom designer. (#S44, Reflection)

A second case of identity shifts was identified in relation to XR design success. Some PSTs (n = 15) described the experience of seeing their VR classroom function as a complete, interactive learning environment as an emotionally significant moment that may have actively reshaped their professional identity. As Extract 12 shows, the material agency of the XR environment, specifically its capacity to function as a responsive space, contributed to participants' identity reconstruction, as Darvin (2025) argued that identity in GenAI-mediated learning emerges as an interactive property among learners, tools, and material environments.

> **Extract 12:** The moment I saw the VR classroom come alive with my group members as avatars and the AI-generated tasks working in the space, I felt really excited and confident. It felt like the tools and I were shaping my identity as a teacher together, not just me deciding who I want to be. (#S13, Interview)

The data thus revealed a bidirectional pattern: successful agentic action built confidence that enabled further risk-taking, while anxiety constrained affordance noticing.

### Theme 7: Authentic co-orchestration during teaching demonstrations

During the teaching demonstration phase, PSTs enacted performative distributed agency, coordinating AI-generated materials, VR environments, peer avatars, and interactive objects simultaneously in real-time pedagogical action (RQ1, RQ2). Many participants (n = 36) reported that the demands of the teaching demonstration differed qualitatively from those of the design phases, as moment-to-moment adaptation replaced iterative design revision as the primary form of professional engagement. When AI-generated tasks proved unclear to peer students, PSTs improvised clarifications. For example, when VR objects failed to respond as designed, PSTs adjusted the task flow. When peer avatars showed disengagement, PSTs who assumed teacher roles adjusted the teaching pace or redirected students' attention. We found that these adaptations required reflection-in-action and integrated previously developed agency into situated pedagogical performance. Extract 13 below illustrates how the distributed character of this orchestration was experienced as a collaborative achievement (also see Fig. 8):

> **Extract 13:** While teaching, I was not acting alone at all. The AI script guided the structure, the VR objects, including ChatGPT embedded into the space, managed part of the task flow, and my peers' avatar movements shaped the timing of everything. Yes. I found we were all orchestrating together, not just me directing from the front. (#S50, Interview)

**Fig. 8.** Co-orchestration among all agents in virtual space.

A second orchestration pattern emerged in relation to unexpected failures in the XR environment during teaching. Some participants (n = 13) described moments when VR objects or interactive elements did not respond as designed as situations that required the most demanding form of distributed agency in the study. As Dillenbourg and Jermann (2010) described, orchestration required the teacher to adapt plans in real time, extended here to a multi-tool context where those plans could be co-authored with AI and expressed through spatial VR layouts. As Extract 14 illustrates, these contingency-response episodes demanded simultaneous diagnostic judgment, real-time task redesign, and communicative management of peer students within the virtual environment, an integration that design preparation alone may not have produced:

> **Extract 14:** I had to adjust mid-lesson when a VR object stopped responding. My agency in that moment was not just me thinking. It was me responding to the tools, the space, and the class all at once as a whole system. I believe that was the hardest and most real moment of the whole course. (#S21, Reflection)

Performative distributed agency, as described in this theme, constituted the culminating form of agentic development in this study, integrating distributed noticing, co-authoring, embodied design, constraint negotiation, collective reasoning, and identity shifts into real-time instructional action. We found that in this way, the GenAI–XR micro-teaching context provided a qualitatively distinct space for agency development.


## 5. Discussion

The seven themes present a developmental sequence of PST agency, from distributed affordance noticing through collaborative co-authoring, constraint negotiation, and identity shifts to performative co-orchestration, indicating that distributed agency in GenAI–XR ecologies is a relational, emergent, and sociomaterial achievement rather than an individual capacity. What follows situates this construct within the broader conversation on distributed agency, SQD2-informed teacher preparation, and GenAI–XR integration, addressing all three RQs. The discussion opens by characterizing the form of distributed agency that emerged (RQ2) before examining its mediating conditions (RQ1) and the constraint dynamics through which it developed (RQ3).

### 5.1. Distributed agency as aggregative entanglement

Addressing RQ2, the seven themes support aggregative entanglement as the distinctive form of distributed agency that emerged: one in which each agent’s role remains identifiable, yet the outcomes produced together appeared to exceed what any single agent could have achieved alone (Sarkio et al., 2023). In this study, throughout the design cycle, PSTs evaluated and redirected AI outputs, ChatGPT generated lesson content at speed, the XR environment shaped spatial decisions that no floor plan had anticipated, and peer collaboration produced collective reasoning that participants described as beyond individual capacity. In this process, each contribution was traceable, yet participants repeatedly described the designed lessons, enacted teaching, and professional identities that emerged as what they could not have produced on their own. This finding suggests that such outcomes are not simply reducible to the sum of individual inputs: this interpretation lies between Schatzki’s (2025) emphasis on traceable individual contribution and Orlikowski’s (2007) view that educational practice is always constitutively entangled, corroborating Sarkio et al.’s (2023) statement that teachers experience classroom environments not as discrete tools and spaces but as an interdependent whole.

This is clearly visible in Themes 2 and 7, one situated in the collaborative design phase and the other in the teaching demonstration, which together span the full developmental sequence. In Theme 2, specifically, PSTs and ChatGPT took on traceable roles: ChatGPT generated draft content while PSTs evaluated and aligned its outputs with their pedagogical goals. The participants described the final lesson designs as outcomes they could not have produced independently, which may suggest that what this practice produces emerges from the relationships among actors rather than from any single contributor (Orlikowski, 2007). Theme 7 extends this pattern to teaching itself: the participants described micro-teaching as emerging from real-time responses to AI scripts, spatial layouts, peer avatars, and their own in-the-moment pedagogical decisions, rather than as what they directed alone. This can be interpreted as what Godwin-Jones (2024) described as an entanglement of humans and non-human actors in GenAI-mediated agency. In both themes, individual contributions remained identifiable, as Schatzki (2025) emphasized, but what participants described as the collective outcome may point to co-production that no single contributor could fully account for.

### 5.2. SQD2 strategies as orchestrating conditions for evaluative agency

Addressing RQ1, the findings suggest how SQD2 strategies mediated distributed agency as conditions within which that agency could develop, rather than as instructional inputs that produced it directly. The developmental sequence across the seven themes, from noticing through co-authoring, embodied design, constraint negotiation, collective reasoning, and identity shifts to performative orchestration, echoes existing developmental models in the literature. Lin et al.'s (2025) three-stage identity development model traced a comparable progression from function-centered exploration through pedagogy-centered critical evaluation to learner-centered creative adaptation, while Aldemir et al.'s (2025) five-stage AI literacy progression mapped the same arc from exploratory noticing through evaluative co-authorship to creative orchestration. These convergences suggest that the trajectory identified here may reflect a broader developmental pattern in how PSTs built professional agency with emerging technologies, rather than an artifact of the specific GenAI–XR context. Our findings extend these studies by specifying how such development was mediated through the sequential activation of SQD2 strategies within a sociomaterial design environment. As Zhou et al. (2025) also reported, ongoing engagement in GenAI-supported design activities could support shifts in PSTs' pedagogical practice. The present study showed how these shifts were associated with distributed, space-based interaction with both human and non-human agents in XR contexts.

The central construct emerging from this trajectory was evaluative agency, the capacity to critically assess, adapt, and purposefully deploy AI outputs within pedagogical goals. Moorhouse et al. (2024) described a shift from treating GenAI as an autonomous content generator to engaging with it as a dialogue partner, and Theme 2 showed a similar move from AI use to co-authorship. Author (2025c) likewise suggested that subject-specific pedagogical knowledge was essential for effective GenAI use, a pattern also evident here, where CLT frameworks grounded evaluative judgment. Rather than operating separately, SQD2 strategies applied to the present study worked together to support this development: modeling and exploration sharpened noticing, collaborative design supported co-authorship and shared reasoning, and authentic teaching with iterative feedback pushed participants toward adaptation, identity reconfiguration, and orchestration (Cabbeke et al., 2025). Thus, the findings in this study showed how distributed agency in GenAI–XR teacher education developed through a sequenced SQD2 cycle, from noticing and co-authorship to shared reasoning, adaptation, identity development, and orchestration.

### 5.3. Compound resistance and the productive failure of integrated ecologies

Addressing RQ3, the findings revealed that PSTs' negotiation of GenAI and XR affordances and constraints produced a resistance accommodation dynamic that extended Högström et al.'s (2024) sociomaterial model from its original dyadic scope of human-tool interaction to the more complex demands of technology ecologies with multiple actors. Högström et al. (2024) particularly stated that when technology functions smoothly, its material agency remains invisible, but when it malfunctions or resists user intentions, its agency becomes visible and demands accommodation. Theme 4 revealed this pattern in a more complex ecology: AI hallucinations forced PSTs to refine prompts and clarify pedagogical intentions, while VR performance failures forced spatial redesign and task simplification. Importantly, these sources of resistance did not arise separately from PSTs’ agentic work. Rather, participants had to coordinate responses across two interacting technologies, ChatGPT and SPOT Virtual, within their pedagogical practice, and this negotiation appeared to enhance their technological knowledge and their pedagogical application in ways not fully captured by Högström et al.’s dyadic framework.

In this study, the combination of GenAI and XR introduced compound resistance, a constraint dynamic absent when either technology is used in isolation. Through this compound dynamic, constraint negotiation became a central mechanism for agency development rather than simple troubleshooting. Author (2025a) showed that integrating AI and XR was associated with higher engagement than using either technology alone, and the present findings suggested a parallel pattern for constraints. When limitations from both technologies interacted, the negotiation demands could exceed what either system alone would require. This extended Chen's (2022) finding that affordances invited but did not determine behavior by showing that constraints could operate in a similar way. In this study, when PSTs encountered GenAI and XR limitations at the same time, each constraint source added to the adaptive demands rather than replacing them, requiring more complex pedagogical judgment than either technology alone would have prompted. Compound resistance, in this sense, is consistent with what Kapur (2016) called productive failure, where unguided struggle generated enhanced conceptual understanding, as opposed to unproductive success, where smooth performance without resistance left little room for exploration. Thus, this view extends both Högström et al.'s (2024) resistance-accommodation model and Kapur's (2016) productive failure framework to educational ecologies integrating multiple technological agents, a condition neither framework was designed to address.

### 5.4. Professional identity shifts and the affective dimension as constitutive features

Addressing RQ2 and RQ3, Theme 6 demonstrated that PSTs' affect and professional identity worked as constitutive dimensions of distributed agency, influencing what PSTs noticed, how they responded to constraints, and whether they persisted through challenges, rather than as outcomes produced by it. The PSTs' identity trajectory described here, from novice tool users to tool negotiators and collaborative designers, parallels Lin et al.'s (2025) three-stage teacher professional identity model, Tan et al.'s (2025) finding that teachers shifted in professional motivation from learner to professional learning leader, and Aldemir et al.'s (2025) finding that PSTs shifted from viewing AI as a productivity tool to treating it as a professional partner. These convergent arguments across different designs, technologies, and learner populations, as well as the findings in this study, indicate that agency in GenAI-mediated learning is an emergent property of interactions among learners, tools, and material environments, and that professional identity is not an individual user's stable trait brought to the learning context but a relational achievement co-produced through it (Darvin, 2025). Zhou et al. (2025) also reported a similar trajectory in in-service contexts under SQD-guided GenAI use, suggesting that such identity shifts can extend to PST training contexts. Tondeur et al.'s (2025) recognition of digital identity development and the affective dimension as new themes of the SQD2 model further suggests that these dimensions are integral to SQD-based teacher preparation, not incidental to it.

The findings in this study identified the dynamic association between affect and agency: positive agentic action (e.g., successful prompting sequences and XR environments "coming alive") built confidence that enabled PSTs to take further risks, while initial anxiety constrained noticing and narrowed the range of agentic moves that PSTs were willing to attempt. This bidirectionality between affect and agency is theoretically significant, as it makes affect not simply a moderator of agency outcomes but an active constituent of the agentic process itself, shaping the PST's identity developmental trajectory from within rather than being conditioned only by external factors (Darvin, 2025; Lin et al., 2025). Dang et al.'s (2025) finding that socio-emotional interactions accounted for some human–AI interactions provides relevant evidence that affect and agency are intertwined in human–AI learning contexts rather than sequential. Regarding Tondeur et al.'s (2025) SQD2 update that acknowledges digital identity development and the affective dimension, the finding in this study specifies the empirical mechanism through which these dimensions operate in the integrated GenAI-XR ecology, tracing how affect and identity dynamics shaped distributed noticing, constraint negotiation, and ultimately performative co-orchestration, and showing that the affective and identity themes in SQD2 are core components of the distributed agency development model (Godwin-Jones, 2024).


## 6. Conclusion

This study examined how SQD2 strategies mediated distributed agency development among EFL PSTs in a 16-week GenAI–XR design project and identified their agency trajectory with seven themes from affordance noticing to performative co-orchestration. The findings positioned distributed agency as an emergent sociomaterial achievement in which human, AI, and spatial contributions were identifiable but their combined outcomes appeared to exceed any single agent's capacity, extending the ecological perspective of teacher agency to GenAI–XR ecologies (Orlikowski, 2007; Priestley et al., 2015; Schatzki, 2025). They also showed that SQD2 did not work through technological knowledge alone but through sustained pedagogical scaffolding, collaborative depth, and authentic technological constraints, as evaluative agency emerged as the core professional competence for aligning AI outputs with pedagogical goals (Godwin-Jones, 2024; Tondeur et al., 2025).

The findings provide implications for future research. Theoretically, this study can advance distributed agency research in three ways. First, it operationalizes distributed agency for GenAI–XR ecologies through both an analytical framework and an empirical model that may provide a basis for replication in future research. Second, it contributed the construct of compound resistance, constraint dynamics emerging from AI–XR interaction that can create productive friction, absent when either technology operates alone, extending Högström et al.'s (2024) resistance-accommodation framework to multi-agent technological ecologies. Thus, future studies may examine how interacting constraints, rather than limitations tied to a single tool, become productive or unproductive across design contexts, varying GenAI scaffolding, XR platform features, and task complexity (Kapur, 2016). Third, it extends Priestley et al.'s (2015) ecological agency model by demonstrating that the resources, affordances, and constraints of the contemporary teaching environment now include autonomous technological agents, such as GenAI systems and GenAI-integrated XR platforms, that can generate content, resist intentions, and reshape design spaces in real time. Future research should specify how degrees of AI autonomy and platform transparency impact teachers' practical-evaluative judgment and distributed agency across contexts (Chen, 2022).

Pedagogically, evaluative agency needs to become an explicit program goal threaded through design, feedback, reflection, and authentic teaching practices rather than providing ethics lessons alone (Author, 2025c; Aldemir et al., 2025). Also, the affective and identity dimensions of technology integration, as recognized in SQD2 (Tondeur et al., 2025), should be treated as core learning objectives rather than side effects; that is, reflection that addresses professional self-concept is foundational, not supplementary (Lin et al., 2025). Finally, upgrading from AI-only use to an integrated GenAI–XR ecology within the SQD2 framework may support PSTs' development of distributed agency in lesson-design projects more effectively than SQD1-based teacher training (Author, 2022). Appendix E synthesizes these implications into seven design principles organized by seven themes in this study and relevant studies from the existing literature.

As with all studies, several limitations should be noted. Since this study examined a single cohort of Korean EFL PSTs using ChatGPT-4o and SPOT Virtual in one institutional context and relied mainly on self-report data, transferability and visibility of real-time agentic processes are limited. Thus, future research should test the model across diverse contexts and tool ecologies and add longitudinal follow-up to examine transfer into practicum and early-career teaching. Also, as this study did not include a comparison condition, the findings cannot establish which design features are necessary rather than merely supportive for the development of distributed agency. Accordingly, future studies should adopt a quasi-experimental approach that compares different sets of SQD2 strategies and GenAI–XR combinations to clarify where the model holds and where it breaks down.

## Appendix A: Sample interview questions

Affordance noticing and constraint recognition (RQ2, RQ3)

1. When you first started working with ChatGPT and SPOT Virtual, what possibilities did you notice that the tools offered for your lesson? Were there things you noticed later that you had initially missed? What helped you see those possibilities?

2. Describe a moment when ChatGPT or the VR environment did not do what you expected or intended. How did your group respond in that moment? How did that experience change the way you worked with the tools afterward?

Spatial design and tool-mediated exploration (RQ1, RQ2)

3. Walk me through one spatial design decision your group made (e.g., room layout, zones, objects, or interaction mechanics). How did that decision shape what was pedagogically possible, and what did it force you to change in the lesson plan?

Human-AI co-authoring and evaluative judgment (RQ1, RQ2, RQ3)

4. Describe one episode of human-AI co-authoring where ChatGPT's output materially changed your lesson (e.g., task flow, language input, assessment). What made you accept, revise, or reject the AI output, and who in the group drove that judgment?

5. Walk me through one key design decision your group made during the lesson planning process. How did you arrive at that decision together? Was the outcome different from what any one of you might have decided individually?

6. Describe a prompting sequence that took multiple iterations before it worked well. What signaled a mismatch between your pedagogical intent and the AI output, and how did your group decide what to change in the next prompt?

Performative agency in teaching demonstrations (RQ2, RQ3)

7. During your teaching demonstration, what required you to think on your feet or adapt in real time? How did that experience of actually teaching in the VR environment feel different from the design phases that came before?

Material resistance and compound constraints (RQ1, RQ2, RQ3)

8. Describe a moment when constraints from ChatGPT and the VR environment interacted (e.g., an AI-generated idea that was difficult to implement in VR, or a VR limitation that changed how you used AI). How did you resolve it, and what did you learn from that negotiation?

Distributed pedagogical judgment and role differentiation (RQ1, RQ2)

9. What roles did group members take on across the project (e.g., prompting, spatial building, scripting, teaching, troubleshooting)? How did role division shape your decision-making, and how did you coordinate when roles pulled in different directions?

Identity and affect (RQ3)

10. Looking back across the whole design cycle, can you identify a moment when your emotions (e.g., anxiety, frustration, confidence) changed what you noticed or attempted? How, if at all, did that moment affect how you see yourself as a future teacher working with GenAI and XR?

## Appendix B: Reflection prompts

1. Describe the most significant challenge you encountered during this phase. How did your group approach it, and what does your response to that challenge reveal about how you are developing as a teacher-designer?

2. In what ways did working with ChatGPT or the VR environment change what you thought was possible in your lesson design? How do you feel about those changes?

3. Reflecting on your role within your group and in relation to the technologies you used, how has your sense of yourself as a future EFL teacher shifted, or not, through this phase of the design work?

| Theme 2 | Cultivate evaluative agency, not just prompting skills: develop PSTs' capacity to critically assess, adapt, and align AI outputs with pedagogical intentions | Moorhouse et al.
