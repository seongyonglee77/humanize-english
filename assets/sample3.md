## Seongyong Lee* and Arum Kim

## Experiences and practices of English as a lingua franca communication in the international university: An integrative view of student voices

https://doi.org/10.1515/applirev-2020-0072

Abstract: Students ' experiences and practices regarding English as a lingua franca (ELF) have been central to the discussion in research on intercultural communication in academia as it provides the basis for English language policy and practices in the international university. To date, however, few previous studies have explored diverse factors for perceptions of ELF communication with a single framework. To fill this gap, this study provides the integrative research model for perceptions and behaviours regarding ELF communication in the international university context, using Theory of Planned Behaviour. It further explores the effect of those factors on actual intentions to engage in ELF communication. Data were collected from 232 questionnaires and seven interviews on the offshore campus of a Hong Kong university in mainland China. Findings showed that factors such as acceptance, social expectations, the sense of control over linguistic knowledge for intercultural communication, and self-esteem had significant effects on intentions. The study concluded that ELF speakers ' decisionmakingprocess to engage in ELF communication is not simply based on individual behaviour, but on social performance in academic communities of practice. This study suggests academic and educational implications for incorporating the ELF perspective into English language policy and practices in the international university.

Keywords: international university, English as a lingua franca, language policy, English medium instruction, intercultural communication

## 1 Introduction

In response to the globalisation of commodities and human resources, major universities around the world have focused on internationalisation of higher education (HE) by defining themselves as the international university (Jenkins 2011, 2014; Sung 2017). This has inevitably made university campuses multilingual and multicultural based on the transnational mobility of faculty and students (Baker 2016). Such an environmental change in HE has raised the status of English as a medium of instruction and academic communication on a multilingual campus. In this regard, English as a lingua franca (ELF) spoken by people from different linguistic backgrounds has been widely discussed as the pedagogical and communicative English model for international universities, which adopt English as a medium of instruction (EMI), in terms of cultural diversity, locally constructed norms, the fl uidity of context, and user perspectives (Baker 2016; Canagarajah 2007).

Recent studies on ELF have focused on university members ' experiences and perceptions regarding ELF use in international universities (Jenkins and Wingate 2015; Sung 2017). Findings from those studies provide an important basis for current issues in HE: (1) pedagogy -English teaching and assessment norms (Fang 2018; Sung 2015); (2) HE language policy: the language model for intercultural communication (Bolton 2011; Fang 2018; Jenkins 2014); (3) HE management: response of international students as customers of HE institutions (HEIs) (Jenkins and Wingate 2015). Previous studies have found positive relationships between exposure, familiarity, awareness, and attitudes in ELF communication (Jenkins 2009; Sung 2016a). Other factors include social expectations from the local academic communities (Canagarajah 2007; Lee and Choe 2019), English ownership (Sung 2015, 2016a), and second language (L2) con fi dence in ELF communication (Jenkins 2014; Sung 2016a, 2017).

To date, however, two important gaps have remained unfilled. Given that diverse factors have been found to influence perceptions of ELF, a paucity of research has been conducted to explore those factors with a single research framework. In addition, previous research has focused on descriptive analysis of belief and attitude, overlooking their impact on actual behaviour to use ELF on the multilingual campus. In response to the gaps, the study is to provide the integrative model for research on students ' perceptions and behaviour regarding the use of ELF in the international university context. The study further aims to explore the effect of those factors on behaviour by statistically examining the sequential relationship among belief, attitude, and behaviour regarding ELF communication. In other words, drawing on the Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen 1991, 2011), the study investigates the decision-making process in which Chinese students reach intentions to adopt their local English variety for ELF communication based on their beliefs and attitudes in the context of the offshore branch campus of an international university. The following two research questions (RQ) guide the study:

RQ1: What factors have significant influences on Chinese international university students ' behavioural intentions regarding ELF communication?

RQ 2: What are the sequential relationships among perceived belief, perceived attitude, and behavioural intention regarding ELF communication?

## 2 Literature review and research model

## 2.1 ELF communication in the international university context

With the rise in the status of English as a global language, ELF has attracted much attention from HE scholars to explore the use of English as a medium of intercultural communication (Fang 2018; Jenkins and Wingate 2015). The pluralistic nature of English in intercultural contact has given a rise to considerable discussion about ELF in academic settings (ELFA). Calling into question the authenticity of the English as a foreign language perspective in multicultural academic contexts, the ELF model conceptualizes the members of the academic community as English users who hold intercultural communication competence, rather than language learners whose English is recognised as being de fi cient due to its deviance from English as a native language (ENL) norms (Baker 2016; Jenkins 2011). In this vein, ELF communication is regarded as social practice of multilingual norms that are collaboratively constructed by speakers with different linguistic backgrounds (Canagarajah 2007).

International universities that adopt the EMI policy provide an ideal setting in which ELF is researched in academic contexts when EMI is conducted in multilingual and multicultural environments (Bolton 2011; Jenkins 2014; Jenkins and Wingate 2015). ELFA researchers have problematised the cultural integration of Anglophone norms into EMI universities, which is believed to be inconsistent with their global quali fi cations (Baker 2016; Jenkins 2014). It is instead suggested that the internationalisation of HE institutions inherently needs the ELF model that values the beliefs and identities of transcultural students on the campus (Baker 2016). Then, the international university can create af fi nity spaces where its members with diverse cultural backgrounds engage in dynamic ELFA communication based on their sense of solidarity and shared communication practices, not on conformity to certain forms (Canagarajah 2007; Jenkins 2014).

However, the dominance of native English speaker (NES) ideology in EMI policies is still found in international universities although this NES centric view may show a considerable distance from what actually happens on such multilingual campuses (Fang 2018). Jenkins (2011) reported that the internationalisation of Anglophone universities was associated with ' the distribution of national British and/or North American English varieties around the globe ' (933). This phenomenon has been perpetuated in their branch campuses in Asia that explicitly proclaim their credentials as international universities by identifying themselves with the host university (Jenkins 2014). However, Jenkins and Wingate (2015) argued that the normative English policy as a one-size- fi t-all approach maintained by international universities in the UK created a sense of unfairness to culturally diverse students.

Accordingly, many publications on EMI policies call for the culturally inclusive English model that reflects the recent discussion of the global spread of English (Fang 2018; Jenkins 2014). Jenkins (2014) argued for accommodating cultural and linguistic assets that international students bring to the university by allowing them to be authentic agents of ELFA communication. To encourage such an ELFA perspective to be broadly adopted by EMI universities in mainland China, Fang (2018) called for teachers ' and students ' recognition of their advantages as multilingual speakers who can bring communicative strategies to negotiation in the international university.

## 2.2 Perceptions of ELF communication in academic settings

The pluralistic view of English has been central to research on perceptions of ELF use for intercultural communication in academic settings (Jenkins and Wingate 2015; Sung 2016a). Sung (2016a) found that students ' positive orientations came from their belief that ELF re fl ected local culture and identities, while NES ideology was related to the perceived dominance of ENL in providing norms. This ambivalent perception is associated with three aspects of ELF communication: perception of non-native English varieties (Sung 2016a), solidarity with the local ELF community (Canagarajah 2007; Jenkins 2011), and L2 con fi dence in ELF communication (Jenkins 2009; Sung 2017).

First, First, attitude toward non-native varieties of English has been one of the most studied topics in previous publications that take a pluralistic view of English

(e.g., Fang 2020; Lee and Choe 2019; Sung 2016a) Those studies have found the potential linear relationship among exposure, familiarity, and cognitive awareness regarding different English varieties. Other studies put forward ELF speakers ' perceived usefulness of their local variety as a signi fi cant factor for their attitudes (Sung2015, 2016a). Tokumoto and Shibata (2011) reported that Malaysian students ' positive perceptions stemmed from their successful use of Malaysian English for ELF communication while Japanese and Korean participants ' negative responses resulted from their beliefs that their English was not intelligible to those from other countries.

Second, perceived social expectations may influence ELF speakers ' attitudes toward non-native English varieties for intercultural communication in the academic context (Jenkins 2009; Sung 2016a). ELF scholars have long used the notion of ' communities of practice ' to explain the contextual factor of ELF communication, including locally constructed English norms, a sense of solidarity based on shared resources of culture and language, and social pressure in linguistic performance (Canagarajah 2007; Jenkins 2014). Canagarajah (2007) described the ELF speech community as emerging through preformative practices activated by English speakers who bring heterogeneous cultures and identities under communicative negotiation in their immediate localities. In the HE context, according to Jenkins (2014), institutional expectations for academic literacy are important factors for students ' attitudes toward adopting fl exible norms in English use. Sung (2017) added that Hong Kong university students ' identity construction in using their local English variety was in fl uenced by other interlocutors ' evaluation.

Finally, L2 confidence in ELF communication has been often mentioned as an influencing factor for attitude in academic settings (Jenkins 2009; Sung 2015, 2017). Sung (2016a) found that ELFA speakers ' con fi dence in cross-cultural communication was based on their consideration of their English as ' real ' English, namely the ownership of English. On the other hand, their native-speaker-centric perspectives were found to have a negative effect on their self-ef fi cacy in ELF communication. In addition to L2 con fi dence, Lee and Choe (2019) found selfesteem as an additional factor for non-native English speakers ' perceived control over their localised English.

However, previous research does not extensively investigate the linear relationship between those factors, attitude, and actual behaviour to use the local English variety for ELF communication. This study therefore examined the effect of factors on ELF speakers ' intentions in the international university context, using the Theory of Planned Behaviour, the social psychological theory, to provide the integrative research framework.

## 2.3 Research model and hypotheses

The Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) was used to identify the belief-attitudebehaviour pathways regarding the use of ELF for intercultural communication from the social psychology perspective (Ajzen 1991, 2011). According to Ajzen ' s (1991) social psychological perspective on human behaviour (see Figure 1), attitude is relevant to an individual ' s positive or negative evaluation of a particular behaviour, and this attitude is in fl uenced by behavioural belief that relates to the probability of expected outcomes being produced by certain behaviours. Subjective norm is concerned with social pressure in a particular social community, which is the function of normative belief as associated with the perceived approval from important referents in the community. Perceived behavioural control, the last predictor of behavioural intention, refers to one ' s perceived control over the source of knowledge to conduct certain behaviours. As in Figure 1, control belief may predict perceived behavioural control based on an individual ' s perception of resources or opportunities for the behaviour. The TPB model has been used as the established framework to test the pathways from belief-attitude factors to individuals ' behavioural intentions in social sciences, including educational technology and language studies (e.g., Cheon et al. 2012; Hsieh et al. 2008; Kim and Pae 2018; Lee and Choe 2019).

The TPB may provide a strong explanation power for research on attitudes toward ELF (cf. Kim and Pae 2018; Lee and Choe 2019). Lee and Choe (2019) explored the sequential effects of Korean university students ' beliefs and attitudes ontheir actual intentions regarding the use of English borrowings as part of Korean English in the Korean speech community. Kim and Pae (2018), using the TPB, reported the signi fi cant effect of Korean high school students ' attitudes toward English learning on their intentions to study English. To advance attitudinal research regarding ELF use (e.g., Fang 2020; Sung 2016a; Tokumoto and Shibata

The first block of the TPB in Figure 1 covers behavioural intention that is the function of attitude, subjective norm, and perceived behavioural control in the second block (Ajzen 2011; Hsieh et al. 2008). Behavioural intention was selected as the fi nal dependent variable because it may directly predict observed behaviour (Ajzen 2011; Cheon et al. 2012) Attitude refers to an evaluation of conducting a particular behaviour, while subjective norm is relevant to social pressure (Ajzen 1991, 2011). Perceived behavioural control refers to an individual ' s perceived control over a behaviour based on a personal source of knowledge or selfcon fi dence. The three constructs have been known to have a positively strong in fl uence on behavioural intention (Ajzen 2011; Cheon et al. 2012). Applying the fi ndings in previous studies to language attitude research, the author developed three hypotheses (see Figure 2):

H2: Participants ' attitude has a positive influence on their intention to use ELFA for intercultural communication.

H3: Participants ' subjective norm has a positive influence on their intention to use ELFA for intercultural communication.

H4: Participants ' perceived behavioural control has a positive influence on their intention to use ELFA for intercultural communication.

The pathway from behavioural belief to attitude was decomposed into perceived acceptance and usefulness based on previous studies of the TPB and perception of ELF communication as in Figure 2: ELF scholars share an idea that the two variables may positively in fl uence attitude toward the use of a local English variety for ELFA communication (Lee and Choe 2019; Sung 2016a; Tokumoto and Shibata 2011). Drawing on the match-up between the TPB and fi ndings in ELF research, Hypothesis 5 and 6 are suggested:

H5: Participants ' perceived acceptance of ELFA has a positive influence on their attitudes.

H6: Participants ' perceived usefulness of ELFA has a positive influence on their attitudes.

Normative belief that refers to social expectations from important referents (Figure 1) is composed of perceived instructor readiness and peer student readiness (Jenkins 2009). The two variables, leading to subjective norm, were examined byparticipants ' perceived expectations from their instructors and peer students for using local English varieties for ELF communication. Two hypotheses were set up to test this assumption:

H7: Perceived instructor readiness for ELFA communication has a positive influence on their subjective norms.

H8: Perceived peer student readiness for ELFA communication has a positive influence on their subjective norms.

Control belief, perceived resources or opportunities for a particular behaviour, was decomposed into perceived L2 confidence and self-esteem guided by previous ELFA

studies of students ' confidence in ELF communication and self-esteem(Jenkins 2009; Lee and Choe 2019). To test the linear pathways between these two constructs and perceived behavioural control, The following two hypotheses were developed:

H9: Perceived self-efficacy toward ELFA communication has a positive influence on their perceived behavioural control.

H10: Perceived self-esteem has a positive influence on their perceived behavioural control.

## 3 Method

## 3.1 Context and participants

To explore cognitive, affective, and behavioural perceptions of Chinese university students at the international university regarding the use of ELF, data were collected from the offshore branch campus of a Hong Kong university in Guangdong, China. The reason for this choice was that the university had a Hong Kong education system, which has a multilingual background, not blindly pursuing an Anglophone curriculum. The medium of instruction is English as a means of communication among speakers of different languages. At the time of data collection, the teaching staff at the university were from 29 foreign countries, and 9% of the students were international students from 12 different countries. These two demographic backgrounds partially provided its students with the multilingual and multicultural environment on campus (Baker 2016; Jenkins 2014).

Prior to the full survey, a pilot study was conducted with 41 students to ensure that all the measurement items in the survey questionnaire obtained validity and reliability. 232 Chinese-English bilingual students (aged 19 -22 years, mean = 20.19, standard deviation [SD] = 0.99; 64 males and 168 females) participated in the fullscale study. Although a convenient sampling method was used, the demographic information in Table 1 shows that the participants from this university were relatively diverse in terms of gender, age, grade, and major, and thus represented the majority of students in the research site.

After the survey, seven students participated in follow-up interviews on a voluntary basis. Table 2 shows their pro fi les: pseudonyms were assigned for the participants to keep their identities con fi dential.

## 3.2 Instrument and data collection

Drawing upon research on attitudes toward different varieties of English and the TPB(Ajzen 2011; Kim and Pae 2018; Lee and Choe 2019), a self-report questionnaire (see Appendix A) was developed to collect the quantitative data that include the participants ' beliefs, attitudes, and behavioural intentions regarding ELFA use for intercultural communication. The questionnaire is composed of two sections: the fi rst section explored the participants ' background information, such as gender, age, grade, and major. In the second section, a 7-point Likert scale was adopted, ranging from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (7), to quantify the participants '

responses to 34 measurement items. These items were designed to measure 10 hypotheses in Figure 2.

The survey instrument was distributed to potential participants in the spring semester of 2019, and a total of 241 questionnaires were collected. After excluding incomplete responses, a total of 232 documents were selected for data analysis. After the survey data were collected, follow-up semi-structured interviews were conducted to explore seven interviewees ' voices about their answers to the survey.

## 3.3 Data analysis

Structural equation modelling (SEM) was used to assess the measurement estimates and to test the sequential pathways among different variables in the research model guided by TPB. SEM is useful to assess the reliability and validity of the measurement constructs, the structural model fit with the collected data, and the path coefficients of the variables (Kline, 2005). SPSS and AMOS 18 were used to assess the measurement model and estimate the structural coef fi cients based on the maximum likelihood estimation method. First, the data were screened to check multivariate normality, using Mardia ' s multivariate kurtosis (Gao et al. 2008). Because the data violated this assumption (kurtosis index: 73.305, Z = 11.137), 6 multivariate outliers were deleted based on the squared Mahalanobis distance (Byrne 2010) to make the data close to normality (Kline 2005), and used 226 responses as the fi nal data set. Second, a con fi rmatory factor analysis (CFA) was conducted to assess the measurement model, using the standardized factor loadings of measurement items, composite reliability (CR), convergent validity, and discriminant validity. Finally, the structural model fi t with the data and its path coef fi cients were measured using SEM. To assess the model fi t with the data, multiple model fi t indices were used, including chi-square statistics and χ 2 /degree of freedom (df), comparative fi t index (CFI), Tucker -Lewis index (TLI), the root mean squared error of approximation (RMSEA), and the standardized root mean residual (SMRM).

Agroundedtheorywasusedtoanalysetheinterviewdataforemergingthemes relevant to the survey results (Strauss and Corbin 2015). Grounded theory is useful to develop a concept or theory ' grounded ' in qualitative data. The grounded theory approach allowed the authors to notice recursive themes related to factors of the interviewees ' affective, cognitive and behavioural attitudes (Ajzen 2011; Jenkins 2011; Lee and Choe 2019). Those recursive themes, such as the interviewees ' attitudes and intentions regarding ELF use, were developed into initial codes that outlined the key ideas repeatedly emerging in relation to research questions. Then, probing in depth with transcripts, the authors further coded the data pertinent to the reasons for their perceptions, grouping initial codes into relevant categories and cross-checking the categories with the original data. Finally, the authors determined fi nal codes to be reported, informed by the survey results and previous literature.

## 4 Findings

## 4.1 Measurement model

The CFA results showed that the measurement model was fair based on assessmentsofconvergentvalidity, reliability, and discriminant validity of the measures, Cronbach ' s α , average variance extracted (AVE), and CR. First, Table 3 shows that standardized factor loadings of all measurement items were over 0.7, suggesting that the measurement items satis fi ed the convergent validity of the measurement model (Gefen et al. 2000). Cronbach α values and the CR values of all variables exceeded 0.7, indicating the high internal consistency among the measurement constructs and good reliability respectively as recommended by Nunnally and Bernstein (1994). All of the AVE values exceeded 0.5, satisfying convergent validity of the measurement model (Fornell and Larcker 1981) Finally, as in Table 4, the square root of the AVE value of each measurement item was higher than the correlation between the items in their rows and columns, displaying the satisfactory discriminant validity (Fornell and Larcker 1981). Overall, the convergent validity, reliability and discriminant validity of all measurement items were con fi rmed for testing the research model.

## 4.2 Structural model fit

Concerning Hypothesis 1, the structural model assessment in Table 5 shows that the values of all fi t indices fell within the range recommended by Kline (2005) ( χ 2 = 1022.469, χ 2 /df = 2.033, CFI = 0.905, TLI = 0.901, RMSEA = 0.068, SMRM=0.061). This indicates that the research model suggested in Figure 2 was a good fi t with the data of the study. All paths except the one between perceived acceptance and attitude were positively signi fi cant ( p &lt; 0.05), and 60% of behavioural intention was explained by three antecedent variables ( R 2 = 0.60). Overall, concerning the fi rst research question, the result implies that the research model based on the TPB accurately predicted Chinese international university students ' intentions to use ELF for intercultural communication.

## 4.3 Attitude to behavioural intention

The results of path coefficient estimation supported Hypotheses 2, 3, and 4: Attitude ( β = 0.23, p &lt; 0.01), subjective norm ( β = 0.47, p &lt; 0.01), and perceived behavioural control ( β =0.31, p &lt; 0.01) were found to have signi fi cant in fl uences on the participants ' intentions to engage in ELF communication in the international university (see Figure 3). This result indicates that subjective norm was the most signi fi cant predictor of the participants ' behavioural intention for ELF communication, followed by perceived behavioural control and subjective norm. The three antecedent variables explained 60% of the participants ' intentions to use ELF for intercultural communication.

The interviewees confirmed the results, displaying high sensitivity to the three factors. First, the participant ' s mixed feelings toward English varieties were relevant to their intentions to use the local variety or to imitate ENL. It is noteworthy that their intentions to adopt Chinese English for intercultural communication were not fully supported by their positive attitudes toward non-native English varieties. Some students reported they would pursue the ENL model because of its effectiveness for intercultural communication. This may explain why attitude had the least impact on intention among the three aforementioned variables.

## Extract 1

Vanessa: I accept different accents because they re fl ect local cultures. However, from my experience, I found it dif fi cult to communicate with Thais in English. So, I am not willing to accept 100% of foreign accents in actual communication situations. I ' ve since tried to use American English more.

Concerning the effect of subjective norm on intention, the environmental factors werereported to allow them to be exposed to non-native varieties of English as well as ENL and to actively engage in cross-cultural communication. The interviewees recognized that their university provided a multicultural environment that helps improve their communication skills in English. Based on this successful experience, they perceived the communication-oriented use of English as social expectations on campus.

## Extract 2

Edith: I think that I can speak English fl uently when I see that professors and students from different cultures use their own English to teach and study at the school. I believe it is a unique culture at our school compared to other local universities.

About the influence of the participants ' perceived control on their intentions, most of them pointed to their communication strategies as the determining factor for successful ELF communication. They valued communicative competence over pronunciation or grammatical correctness because they considered fluency to be the suggestive requirement of proficient English users. They further mentioned that the more confidence they had in their communication skills, the more likely they would be to use their local English variety in ELF communication.

## Extract 3

John: I think fl uency is more important to talk to foreigners, and I think there is no problem with my communication skills. If communication problems occur due to different accents or pronunciations, I think it can be overcome through various methods.

Overall, survey and interview data showed the significant influence of three attitudinal variables on the intention to engage in ELF communication. To be more specific, the combined effects of the participants ' mixed feelings toward the local and other varieties of English, their consciousness of multilingual contexts, and their confidence in communication skills were found on their engagement in actual communication with those from other cultures.

## 4.4 Belief to attitude

Concerning the two antecedent variables of attitude, the regression path from perceived acceptance to attitude was positively significant, supporting Hypothesis 5 ( β = 0.66, p &lt; 0.01). Hypothesis 6 was not supported because the path from perceived usefulness to attitude was not signi fi cant ( β = 0.02, p &gt; 0.05). The results indicate that Chinese university students ' attitudes toward the use of ELF on the international campus were signi fi cantly in fl uenced by their perceived acceptance of different varieties of English, but their perceived usefulness was not a meaningful factor for their attitudes.

The participants ' strong sensitivity to perceived acceptance rather than to perceived usefulness was confirmed in their interviews. A possible explanation is that although multilingual experiences provided them with open-mindedness to linguistic diversity on the international campus, a few participants favoured American or British English in a formal setting as it reportedly represented a professional image. Some students, such as John, still held stronger native speaker ideologies regarding effective communication among those whose first languages are different. Such NES ideology may partially explain the weak regression from perceived usefulness to attitude.

## Extract 4

Matthew: Personally, I can accept other varieties of English … The local English looks fi ne in a casual conversation, but in formal situations, I believe using British or American English makes me look more professional.

## Extract 5

John: I think using American or British English may help us to some extent with effective communication. This is because American or British English is dominant worldwide in English education.

Regarding Hypothesis 7 and 8, both perceived instructor readiness ( β = 0.12, p &lt; 0.05) and perceived peer student readiness ( β = 0.86, p &lt; 0.01) were found to be determining variables of subjective norm. However, the result in Figure 3 shows that perceived peer student readiness was identi fi ed as a stronger indicator of social pressure to engage in ELF communication than perceived instructor readiness.

Several interview participants reported that opportunities to speak with foreign instructors contributed to the formation of social expectations for linguistic diversity; that is to say, acceptance of different English varieties and use of their local English. However, as in Katherine ' s report, some supported the normativeness of American English for intercultural communication.

## Extract 6

Katherine: I think speaking with professors from different countries on campus is a great opportunity to improve communication skills. However, when I talk to my English teachers, I usually care much about my pronunciation because I think they sometimes can ' t understand my English due to my poor pronunciation.

Most of the interviewees shared the idea that their peer students held positive attitudes toward the use of their local English, and such a sociolinguistic environment affected their linguistic openness. As Cynthia mentioned below, this attitude allowed them to focus on communication rather than grammar or accent.

## Extract 7

Cynthia: Students at my school are open to a variety of English styles and have an open attitude to use their English with con fi dence. Because of this open atmosphere, I don ' t care much about grammar or pronunciation, but focus on communication.

In sum, while they often considered NES teachers to be norm providers of their English, they regarded other students as communication partners with equal social status. This analysis may explain why the survey participants were more sensitive to their peer students ' perceptions of their local English than those of their instructors ' .

Finally, regarding Hypothesis 9 and 10 (Figure 3), perceived L2 con fi dence ( β = 0.52, p &lt; 0.01), and perceived self-esteem to perceived behavioural control ( β = 0.38, p &lt; 0.01) were both signi fi cant although the former was slightly more in fl uential to perceived behavioural control than the latter. This indicates that Chinese international university students recognized con fi dence in their English as the main source of their control over linguistic knowledge for intercultural communication.

Interviewees mentioned much more about perceived L2 confidence than perceived self-esteem, which may explain the result in Figure 3. To Cynthia, her perception of con fi dence in her English was connected to the sense of control over English communication. Such con fi dence stemmed from her vocabulary and grammatical knowledge rather than her accent.

Several interviewees pointed to the importance of self-esteem as the source of their control over communication with those from different cultures. ## 5 Discussion

This study tested multiple factors of ELF communication intentions in the international university context from the social psychological perspective. For the first research question, the structural model fit assessment (Hypothesis 1) confirmed the goodness of fit for the research model, suggesting the authenticity of the model to explore behavioural attitudes toward ELF communication in the given context. The path coefficients analysis revealed that three variables, such as sociolinguistic attitudes, social pressure, and their perceived control over linguistic knowledge, had significant influences on intention, suggesting that the factors be considered for attitudinal research on ELF communication.

The second research question explored the sequential relationships among different variables of belief, attitude, and intention. First, subjective norm was the most influential factor ( β = 0.47, p &lt; 0.01) among the three factors for intention. This result indicates that ELF users in the multilingual HEIs were notably sensitive to social expectations for their decisions to use the local English variety for intercultural communication. This fi nding reminds us of the importance of communities of practice that provide important implications for research on the contextual aspects of ELF communication (Canagarajah 2007; House 2003; Jenkins 2011). As Jenkins (2011) argued, EFL speakers ' practices of languages are bound to be ' contextually appropriate ' in the communities of imagination (932). In other words, a suitable English model is mutually determined in interactions by members of the local ELF community (Canagarajah 2007). Thus, as far as intercultural communication is concerned, ELF speakers ' decision-making process as to which English model to choose is not simply based on an individual behaviour, but on social performance (Canagarajah 2007; Jenkins and Wingate 2015).

Concerning the two antecedent variables of subjective norm, the effect of perceived instructor readiness was significantly stronger than that of perceived peer student readiness. On the one hand, this result corroborates previous studies that highlighted the role of teachers and English curriculum in forming students ' perceptions (Jenkins 2009; Lee and Choe 2019). On the other hand, it also suggests that the role of peer students in collaboratively creating English norms is especially important in an international university.

The least influence of attitude on the behavioural intention ( β = 0.23, p &lt; 0.01) implies that an individual ' s attitude is not the only factor for their actual behaviour although the affective and cognitive attitude is the research topic most covered in previous publications (Jenkins 2009; Lee and Choe 2019; Sung 2016a). The other reason can be found in some interviewees ' cognitive dissonance regarding the different varieties of English. Most of them accepted the authenticity of non-native English varieties as independent entities, but some of them acknowledged the superiority of ENL for intercultural communication. Such mixed feelings were due to the contextual factors, including the formality of a conversation and the nativeness of conversation partners. The results resonate with several studies by Sung (2016b, 2017), arguing for the importance of context in relation to identity construction in ELF communication.

Finally, it is noteworthy that the participants ' perceptions of control over the source of linguistic knowledge were another significant factor for their intentions to maintain the local English variety ( β = 0.31, p &lt; 0.01). In particular, although previous publications on perceptions of the local English variety reported that nonnative speakers perceived a lack of con fi dence (Sung 2015; Tokumoto and Shibata 2011), whether this feeling of being deviant from ENL actually affects ELF communication has not been fully addressed. Thus, this study presented the signi fi cance of sequential pathways from L2 con fi dence and self-esteem to perceived control of one ' s linguistic sources to intentions for ELF communication in the international university.

## 6 Conclusion and implications

The study provided the integrative research model based on the TPB framework by investigating diverse factors affecting Chinese students ' decision-making process regarding ELF communication in the international university. The results showed that the participants ' intentions to use the local variety of English for intercultural communication were positively influenced directly by attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control, and indirectly by six constructs of perceived belief.

The findings suggest some academic and educational implications. The first academic implication is that future research on ELF communication may consider multiple factors as the determinants of actual behaviour based on the integrative research perspective. In this vein, the author suggests the social psychological model to address both individual and social aspects of English use, namely micro and macro language practices, in the multilingual HEIs (Canagarajah 2007; House 2003). For example, House (2003) noted the necessity to pay attention to a social (macro) perspective of ELF use, focussing on the fl uidity of norms in ELF communities of practice, as well as an individual (micro) aspect of individual ELF speakers ' attitudes and pragmatic skills. Canagarajah (2007: 928) added that the social psychological aspect of ELF acquisition is important as ELF is ' not a product located in the mind of the speaker; it is a form of social action. ' Second, the research model may be extended by adding ENL ideologies as an antecedent variable of attitude and intention. As shown in the interview data, ENL ideologies may have explained the relatively weak regression pathways from attitude to intention (Jenkins 2014; Sung 2016a).

Regarding educational implications, English teaching practices should not only be concerned with increasing familiarity and awareness, but also with the experience of ELF communication practices in a multilingual environment as recommendedbySung(2015,2016a). This suggestion is supported by the interview data, revealing that the simple exposure to different varieties of English did not help them fully escape from ENL ideologies (Lee and Choe 2019; Sung 2016a). Second, to foster such practical experiences, it is necessary to create a culturally inclusive campus based on the ELF perspective where students can contribute to determining pragmatic norms of English (Fang, 2018; Sung 2016b). Fang (2018) emphasized the importance of acknowledging international students ' multicultural backgrounds that may culturally enrich EMI universities from the ELF perspective. Participation in ELF interactions on a multilingual campus, as Sung (2016b: 313) suggested, can encourage students to bring their diverse linguistic and cultural resources to negotiation and contribute to creating ' culturally inclusive space ' in the international university.

As with all studies, several limitations need to be acknowledged. First, since the current study was conducted in an international university in Mainland China, its findings may not be generalised in other contexts. As many publications pointed to the importance of context in attitude studies (Jenkins 2009; Sung 2016a), future research should examine the application of the study in different educational settings. Second, given that exposure to different varieties of English is an important factor for ELF users ' attitudes, participants in this study may have different exposures to English since the study recruited all grades. Thus, future studies are suggested to compare different grades or to be limited to only one grade.
